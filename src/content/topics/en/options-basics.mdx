A stock either goes up or it doesn't, and you own it or you don't. Options blow that binary wide open. An option is a **contract that gives you the right — but never the obligation — to buy or sell something at a price you lock in today**, before a deadline. That single twist (right, not obligation) bends the payoff into a hockey stick: capped loss on one side, open-ended upside on the other. It's the closest thing finance has to buying insurance, and to buying a lottery ticket, depending on which side of the contract you stand on.

This topic builds the language from nothing. First, **what an option actually is** — calls vs puts, strike, premium, expiry, the holder who pays and the writer who gets paid. Then the centrepiece every options trader sketches on a napkin: **payoff and profit diagrams**, where the breakeven sits, and why one side's gain is exactly the other's loss. From there we crack open the premium into **intrinsic value plus time value**, learn the vocabulary of **moneyness** (in, at, out of the money), and watch time value **melt toward expiry** — the theta decay that quietly works against every option buyer. Next, the **six inputs that move a premium** (spot, strike, time, volatility, rates, dividends) and why volatility is the one traders obsess over. We finish by snapping the pieces together into the **first real strategies** — the covered call, the protective put, the bull call spread, and the straddle — so you can shape a payoff to a view instead of just betting up or down. No Black–Scholes yet; just the ideas every pricing model is built to formalise.
