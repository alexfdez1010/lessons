You already know money has a clock — a dollar today beats a dollar tomorrow because
today's dollar can go earn something. This topic is about *that earning*: *interest*,
the price money charges for time. Sounds simple, until you notice that almost every
rate you'll ever be quoted is misleading on purpose. A loan advertised at "12%" can
quietly cost you more. A savings account paying "12%" can quietly pay you more. And a
bond yielding "5%" can leave you **poorer** than when you started.

This topic untangles all three traps. First **simple vs compound interest** — the
difference between earning on your principal and earning on your interest too, and why
*how often* it compounds changes the answer. Then **APR vs APY** — the quoted rate
versus the rate you actually get once compounding is baked in, and why lenders and
savers quote whichever one flatters them. Finally **nominal vs real** — stripping out
inflation to see what your money can actually *buy*, because a number going up isn't
the same as wealth going up. Get these three and you'll never again take a rate at
face value.
