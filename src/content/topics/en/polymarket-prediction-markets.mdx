Most markets price *things* — a share of Apple, an ounce of gold, a barrel of oil. A **prediction market** prices something stranger and more useful: **the probability that a future event happens**. You buy a share that pays exactly **$1 if you're right and $0 if you're wrong**, and the price you pay — somewhere between a penny and 99 cents — *is the crowd's probability* that the event occurs. Pay 62¢ for "YES" and the market is telling you it thinks the event is 62% likely. That single idea — **price equals probability** — is the spine of this whole course, and it turns betting on the world into a quantitative discipline you already have the tools for.

We start at the concept: what a prediction market is, why a $1/$0 payout makes the price a probability, and why a crowd betting real money tends to aggregate scattered information into a sharper forecast than any pundit. Then we go **inside Polymarket** — the largest on-chain prediction market — end to end: settlement in **USDC on Polygon**, outcome tokens that come in **complete $1 sets** (one YES + one NO), **minting** a set from a dollar and **merging** it back, and the **central limit order book (CLOB)** where YES shares trade, with its spread, depth, and slippage. Next, the part everyone underestimates: **resolution**. A market is only as trustworthy as the thing that decides who was right, and Polymarket leans on **UMA's optimistic oracle** — a proposer posts an outcome with a bond, a liveness window lets anyone dispute, and a disputed market escalates to a token-holder vote. Get this wrong and a "winning" share pays nothing.

From mechanics we turn to **edge**. Because price is probability, you can score the market the way you'd score any forecaster: a **calibration plot** asks whether events priced at 30¢ actually happen 30% of the time, and reveals the persistent **favorite–longshot bias** — longshots run rich, heavy favorites run cheap. We'll nail the cleanest free lunch in the building, **binary arbitrage**: a YES and a NO together redeem for $1, so whenever they trade for less (or more) than a dollar, there's a locked profit — until fees eat it. Finally we put your **bayesian-finance** and **kelly-and-cagr** training to work: form a probability the *Bayesian* way, compare it to the price to measure your edge, and **size the bet with Kelly on a binary** — never forgetting that the edge can be an illusion if you're less calibrated than the crowd. We close on the risks that are unique to this venue: ambiguous resolution, oracle disputes, thin-book illiquidity, and the regulatory/geofencing reality.

This is an expert course, and it assumes you arrive with two things already sharp: **Bayesian updating** (to form your own probability) and **Kelly sizing** (to bet it). Bring them — every lesson cashes them out on a live, on-chain market where being right pays a dollar and being wrong pays nothing.
