There are two ways to talk about probability, and most of finance is taught in the wrong one. The **frequentist** says a probability is a long-run frequency — flip the coin a million times and count. That works beautifully when you *can* repeat the experiment a million times. But you cannot rerun 2008. You cannot collect a thousand independent copies of *this* market, with *this* news, *today*. You get one history, a fistful of noisy data, and a head full of prior beliefs — and you have to act anyway. That is the **Bayesian** world, where a probability is a **degree of belief**, and the central question is not "how often?" but "**given what I knew and what I just saw, what should I believe now?**"

This topic builds the full apparatus for answering that question, from intuition to real practitioner tools. It opens with the three objects every Bayesian argument is made of — the **prior** (what you believed before), the **likelihood** (how probable the data is under each hypothesis), and the **posterior** (your updated belief) — and the rule that fuses them. Then it confronts the single most expensive mistake in all of quantitative trading: **confusing P(data given hypothesis) with P(hypothesis given data)**. A signal that fires on 90% of real edges is *not* a signal that's right 90% of the time — and once you reason in **natural frequencies** ("of 1000 signals…"), you'll see exactly how a rare edge plus a good test still produces mostly false alarms. That's the **base-rate fallacy**, and it has emptied more accounts than any crash.

From there we get constructive. **Conjugate priors** make updating a one-line arithmetic operation: a belief about a strategy's win rate, carried in a **Beta** distribution, updates to a sharper belief by *adding the wins and losses* — today's posterior is tomorrow's prior, and the prior itself behaves like pseudo-data. For continuous returns the **Normal–Normal** update reveals the deep truth that a posterior mean is a **precision-weighted average** of your prior and your data: noisy data barely moves you, abundant data takes over, and you always end up *more* certain than you started. That same machinery, pointed at portfolios, becomes **shrinkage** — the James–Stein result that pulling noisy estimates toward a common center beats trusting them — and the **Black–Litterman model**, which blends the market's equilibrium returns (the prior) with an investor's views (the data) into a posterior that finally tames the "error-maximizing" optimizer.

We finish with how to *read* a posterior and how to *get* one when no formula will give it to you. A **credible interval** says the thing everyone wrongly wishes a confidence interval said — "there's a 95% probability the true value is in this range" — and we draw the line between the two cleanly enough that you'll never again say it backwards. And when the posterior has no closed form, **Markov-chain Monte Carlo** samples it anyway: propose, compare, accept-or-stay, repeat, and the histogram of where the chain wanders *is* the posterior — the normalizing constant you couldn't compute quietly cancels out. By the end you will think in priors and posteriors by reflex, size your confidence to your evidence, and never again mistake a loud signal for a true one.
