You can buy a stock by tapping a green button and never asking what you actually bought. But a share is a claim on a *business* — its assets, its debts, and above all its future cash. The investors who consistently win don't guess at price charts; they **open the books**, work out what the business earns and owns, and decide what that stream of cash is worth today. This course teaches that craft, from the first line of a balance sheet to a complete discounted-cash-flow valuation.

We build it brick by brick. This course covers:

- **The three statements** — the **balance sheet** (what a company owns and owes at a moment), the **income statement** (what it earned over a period), and the **cash-flow statement** (where the cash actually went) — and the iron identities that govern each.
- **How the three link** — net income flows into retained earnings, the cash-flow statement reconciles profit to cash, and every change on one statement echoes on the others. Break one and the model won't balance.
- **Margins, ratios and EPS** — gross/operating/net margins, return on equity decomposed by **DuPont**, debt/equity and interest coverage, and exactly what "earnings per share" counts (and quietly leaves out).
- **Valuation multiples** — P/E, EV/EBITDA and P/B: what each compares, why enterprise value beats market cap for leveraged firms, and how to read a multiple without being fooled by one.
- **Discounted cash flow** — projecting free cash flow, discounting it at the cost of capital, the terminal value that dominates the answer, and a sensitivity table that shows how fragile the "precise" number really is.
- **Growth vs value, and red flags** — the two great investing styles, and the accounting tricks — channel stuffing, capitalised costs, ballooning receivables — that make weak businesses look strong.

By the end you won't just own shares — you'll be able to read a company's filings, compute what the business is worth, and explain in numbers why the market price is fair, cheap, or a trap.
