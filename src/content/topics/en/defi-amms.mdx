A normal exchange matches buyers with sellers in an **order book**: someone has to be selling before you can buy. That works beautifully on a fast central server — and falls apart on a blockchain, where every order update would be a slow, expensive transaction and there's nobody to run the matching engine. So decentralized exchanges threw the order book out and replaced it with something stranger and simpler: a **pool of two tokens and a formula** that prices every trade automatically. That's an **Automated Market Maker (AMM)**, and it is the engine under nearly all on-chain trading.

This topic takes the machine apart bolt by bolt. We start with **why AMMs exist** and how trading-against-a-pool differs from trading-against-a-person. Then the heart of it: the **constant-product formula x·y=k**, the one equation that sets every price — and why a trade just walks the pool along a curve. From there, the two sides of the table: becoming a **liquidity provider**, what **LP tokens** are, and how fees accrue to your share; and as a trader, **slippage and price impact** — why a big trade gets a worse price and what "slippage tolerance" actually protects you from. We finish with the concept that trips up every new LP: **impermanent loss**, the closed-form that predicts it, and the honest accounting of when providing liquidity beats just holding. By the end, "I swapped on Uniswap" stops being magic and becomes arithmetic you can do yourself.
