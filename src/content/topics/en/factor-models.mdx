You already know CAPM: one number, beta, supposedly explains every asset's expected return. There's a problem. By the early 1990s the evidence was overwhelming that beta *doesn't* do the job — small stocks, cheap stocks, and recent winners all earned far more than their betas could justify. CAPM wasn't just imperfect; for the cross-section of stocks it was, in Fama and French's hands, **broken**. The fix wasn't to throw out the idea of priced risk — it was to admit there is **more than one kind** of it.

That is the entire premise of factor models: a stock's return is driven by its exposure to several systematic, rewarded risks — the market, yes, but also **size**, **value**, **momentum**, **profitability**, and **investment** — each with its own price tag. Get the exposures (the *loadings*) by regression, multiply each by its long-run *premium*, add them up, and you have explained most of the return before a single word about manager skill. Whatever is left over — the regression intercept — is the **alpha**. And the brutal lesson of this whole field is that once you measure it honestly, **most alpha evaporates**: it was hidden factor beta all along.

This course builds that machinery from the ground up and then turns it skeptical. **From CAPM to factors** shows exactly which anomalies broke the one-factor world and what a "factor" even is. **The Fama–French three-factor model** adds size and value, and shows how SMB and HML are built from a 2×3 sort of every stock on the exchange. **Momentum and the five-factor model** adds the strongest and scariest factor (momentum, with its rare catastrophic crashes) plus profitability and investment, and meets the q-factor challenger. **Estimating factor exposures** is the econometrics: time-series regressions for one asset's loadings and alpha, cross-sectional regressions and the Fama–MacBeth two-pass for whether a factor is *priced* at all. **Alpha vs factor exposure** decomposes a real fund's return into factor contributions plus a residual, and shows why "beating the S&P" usually isn't skill. Finally, **smart beta and the factor zoo** confronts the uncomfortable truth: there are 300+ published factors, most are statistical mirages, and the real ones lose roughly a third to a half of their premium the moment they're published and everyone piles in.

By the end you will be able to read a fund's factor fingerprint, run and interpret a factor regression, separate genuine skill from a clever tilt, and treat any shiny new "factor" with the exact amount of suspicion it deserves. This is the toolkit professional allocators actually use to decide whether a manager is worth paying — and whether a strategy is real or just overfit to the past.
