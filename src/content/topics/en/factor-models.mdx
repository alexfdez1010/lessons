CAPM says one number, beta, explains every asset's expected return — and by the early 1990s that story was **broken**: small stocks, cheap stocks, and recent winners all earned far more than their betas could justify. Factor models are the fix, admitting there's *more than one kind* of priced risk, and the brutal lesson is that once you measure honestly, **most "alpha" evaporates** — it was hidden factor beta all along.

Here's what you'll build, from the machinery up to the skepticism:

- **From CAPM to factors** — which anomalies broke the one-factor world, and what a "factor" even is.
- **The Fama–French three-factor model** — adds size and value, and how SMB and HML come from a 2×3 sort of every stock on the exchange.
- **Momentum and the five-factor model** — the strongest, scariest factor (momentum, with its rare catastrophic crashes), plus profitability and investment, and the q-factor challenger.
- **Estimating factor exposures** — the econometrics: time-series regressions for an asset's loadings and alpha, plus cross-sectional and Fama–MacBeth two-pass tests of whether a factor is *priced* at all.
- **Alpha vs factor exposure** — decompose a real fund's return into factor contributions plus a residual, and see why "beating the S&P" usually isn't skill.
- **Smart beta and the factor zoo** — the 300+ published factors, why most are statistical mirages, and how the real ones lose a third to a half of their premium once everyone piles in.

By the end you'll read a fund's factor fingerprint, run a factor regression, tell genuine skill from a clever tilt, and give any shiny new "factor" exactly the suspicion it deserves — the toolkit professional allocators actually use to decide whether a manager is worth paying.
