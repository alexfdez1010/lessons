Market Microstructure taught you to *read* the order book; Algorithmic Trading taught you to *cross* the spread cheaply. This course flips you to the other side of every trade: the **market maker** who posts the two prices everyone else trades against, and earns the spread for standing there.

It looks like free money — buy at the bid, sell at the ask, pocket the difference, repeat a million times a day. It isn't. Two forces are constantly trying to bleed the maker dry: **inventory risk** (every fill leaves you holding a position that can move against you) and **adverse selection** (the people most eager to trade with you are exactly the ones who know something you don't). The whole craft is quoting wide enough, and skewing cleverly enough, to earn more spread than those two forces take back — while a pack of faster firms races you for every profitable quote.

You'll learn, from the inside:

- **The market-making P&L** — the exact three-way decomposition (spread capture − adverse selection − inventory cost) that says whether a quoting business actually makes money.
- **The Avellaneda–Stoikov model** — the canonical optimal-quoting framework: the **reservation price**, the **optimal spread**, and how risk aversion, volatility, and time-to-close set them.
- **Inventory risk & quote skewing** — why the maker shades *both* quotes toward the side that flattens it, turning a wandering position into a mean-reverting one.
- **Adverse selection & Glosten–Milgrom** — the model where the spread exists *purely* because some traders are informed, and quotes update with the information their orders reveal.
- **Queue position & order-book dynamics** — why *where you stand* in the queue is half the game, and how fill probability and the cancel/replace race actually work.
- **Latency & the speed arms race** — colocation, microwave towers, sniping, and the billion-dollar question of whether being one microsecond faster is worth it.
- **Maker-taker & rebate economics** — how exchange fee schedules, rebates, and tiers quietly reshape where and how makers quote.

Finish this and the on-chain version — `onchain-arbitrage-and-cross-dex-mev` — will read like the same game played on a slower, more transparent, and far stranger venue.
