You already know how to measure the risk of *one* asset — its volatility, its drawdown, its Sharpe ratio. But a bank holds **thousands** of positions: stocks, bonds, currencies, options, swaps, all tangled together by correlation. The board, the regulator and the risk desk don't want a thousand volatilities. They want **one number**, in dollars, that answers a brutally simple question: *how bad could tomorrow be?*

**Value at Risk (VaR)** is that number. It compresses an entire portfolio's risk into a single sentence — *"we are 99% confident we will not lose more than \$4.2 million over the next day"* — and for three decades it has been the lingua franca of risk management, written into the **Basel** banking rules and quoted in every trading-floor risk report on Earth. It's seductive precisely because it's so compact: a probability, a horizon, a dollar figure, done.

This topic builds that number from scratch, then teaches you to distrust it intelligently. First, **what VaR actually says** — the three ingredients (horizon, confidence, amount) and the crucial things it deliberately *doesn't* tell you. Then the **three engines** that produce it: **historical simulation** (replay the past), the **parametric / variance–covariance** method (assume a bell curve and use algebra), and **Monte Carlo** (simulate thousands of synthetic tomorrows) — each with its own worked numbers, assumptions, and failure modes. We'll **scale VaR across time** with the square-root-of-time rule and see when it breaks, then meet VaR's sharper successor — **Expected Shortfall (CVaR)** — which answers the question VaR refuses to: *when you do breach the line, how bad is it on average?* Finally, we **backtest**: count the days reality punched through your VaR, run the **Kupiec test** and the **Basel traffic light**, and stare directly at the thing that humbled VaR in 2008 — **fat tails** and the false comfort of a single confident number.

This is the keystone of the quant-risk ladder: every later topic — Monte Carlo finance, extreme-value theory, risk of ruin — sharpens a blade you first pick up here. By the end you'll be able to compute a VaR three ways, average its tail, scale it, backtest it, and explain — with numbers — exactly when it's lying to you.
