Stochastic processes handed you the rulebook for how prices wander — the drifts, the diffusions, the martingales. But a rulebook is only theory until you point it at real data. **Time-series finance is the forensics**: you take an actual return series, ask what it remembers and what it forgets, fit the rules to it, forecast both the next move *and* the next storm, and — the part that separates professionals from the people who blow up — refuse to be fooled by a backtest that looks too good to be true. This is where the abstract machinery of randomness-through-time becomes a working pipeline you can run on tonight's closing prices.

Everything starts with one unglamorous but non-negotiable idea: **stationarity**. Every technique in this topic quietly assumes the series you feed it behaves *consistently* over time — its mean and spread don't go wandering off as the calendar turns. The cruel joke is that the most obvious financial series of all, a stock's **price**, flunks that assumption badly: it carries a **unit root**, so shocks never decay and its variance grows without bound. A tiny transformation — the **return** (the differenced log price) — sails through. So the first lesson pins down exactly why prices are non-stationary and returns roughly aren't, how the **augmented Dickey–Fuller test** detects a unit root, and how logs and differencing turn an un-modelable series into a modelable one. Get this wrong and everything downstream is built on sand.

With a stationary series in hand, we ask the obvious question: does today's return tell you anything about tomorrow's? **Autocorrelation** is the measurement, and its two diagnostic plots — the **ACF** and the **PACF** — are the fingerprint readers of the whole field. You'll learn to judge whether any structure is real against the **white-noise band** ($\pm 2/\sqrt{n}$), to read off which model a series needs (AR cuts off in the PACF, MA in the ACF), and to use the **Ljung–Box test** to confirm a fitted model has wrung out all the structure. From there we build the machines: **AR** (today depends on its own past values), **MA** (today depends on past shocks), and their combination into the **ARIMA(p,d,q)** family — the workhorse of classical forecasting — choosing the order honestly with AIC/BIC and validating on the residuals.

But ARIMA models only the *mean*, and it assumes the variance is constant — which is spectacularly false. Real markets have calm stretches and violent stretches that **cluster** together, one of the most robust facts in all of finance. So we pivot to modeling *risk itself*: the **ARCH** insight that variance depends on recent shocks, the **GARCH(1,1)** model that forecasts variance with just three parameters (a baseline, a reaction to news, and the persistence of yesterday's volatility), and the all-important **persistence** $\alpha + \beta$ that sets how slowly a volatility shock fades and what long-run level it reverts to. Then its famous simplified cousin: the **exponentially weighted moving average (EWMA)**, the one-parameter estimator behind J.P. Morgan's **RiskMetrics** and the Value-at-Risk numbers that every trading desk lives by — and exactly why a fixed $\lambda = 0.94$ beat a fitted GARCH for firm-wide, auditable, daily risk.

The topic closes on the most dangerous lesson of all: **how to not fool yourself**. A backtest done carelessly is a machine for generating confident, profitable-looking, completely worthless strategies. You'll learn the three deadliest traps — **look-ahead bias** (using tomorrow's information today), **overfitting** (memorizing noise instead of signal), and **multiple testing / data-snooping** (reporting the luckiest of a thousand tries) — and the discipline that defends against them: parsimony, honest out-of-sample holdouts, deflating for the number of trials, and **walk-forward analysis** that mimics live trading across many regimes. A **graded final exam** then runs the whole pipeline back at you, one locked question at a time. Master this topic and you can take a raw price series all the way to a validated, risk-aware model — and, just as importantly, know precisely when a dazzling result deserves your suspicion rather than your capital.
