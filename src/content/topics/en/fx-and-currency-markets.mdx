Every other market you've met — stocks, bonds, futures — is *priced in* a currency.
This one **is** the currency. The foreign-exchange (FX) market is where one nation's
money is swapped for another's, and at roughly **\$7.5 trillion a day** it is, by a
wide margin, the largest and most liquid market on the planet. It never really
closes, no single exchange owns it, and the prices it sets quietly ripple through
every import bill, every overseas investment, and every central bank's nightmares.

It also runs on its own grammar. A stock has *a* price; a currency only has a price
*against another currency*, so quotes come in **pairs** and you have to know which
side is which. Forwards aren't priced by storage and dividends here — they're pinned
almost entirely by the **gap between two countries' interest rates**, a piece of
no-arbitrage logic so tight it has its own name. And the players include something no
other market has: **central banks**, who can print the thing being traded.

You'll learn:

- **How a quote works** — base vs quote currency, pips, bid/ask spreads, and how to
  build a cross rate when no direct quote exists.
- **Spot vs forward FX** — T+2 settlement, forward points, FX swaps, and
  non-deliverable forwards for currencies you can't freely move.
- **Covered interest parity** — the airtight arbitrage that turns the interest-rate
  differential into the forward price, with no forecasting required.
- **Uncovered parity & the carry trade** — why borrowing cheap currencies to hold
  high-yielding ones earns a steady premium, why theory says it shouldn't, and why
  it periodically detonates.
- **Central banks, pegs & crises** — intervention, fixed vs floating regimes, the
  *impossible trinity*, and the anatomy of a currency crisis from 1992 to 1997.
- **Currency in a portfolio** — how unhedged foreign holdings smuggle in FX risk,
  what hedging actually costs, and when to bother.

Finish this and the global financial system stops looking like a wall of tickers and
starts looking like one connected machine — where an interest-rate decision in one
capital moves the price of money everywhere else.
