Pick the single best stock you can and you still have a problem: you've bet everything on one company's luck. Modern Portfolio Theory — the framework Harry Markowitz launched in 1952 and won a Nobel for — makes that intuition precise. Its headline result is almost suspicious: by spreading money across assets that don't move in perfect lockstep, you can cut a portfolio's risk **without** cutting its expected return. That's why diversification is called the only free lunch in finance — and this topic serves it one course at a time.

We build the machine from the ground up. First, **why diversification works** — and the crucial split between the company-specific risk you can wash out and the market risk you're stuck with. Then the engine that makes it tick: **correlation and covariance**, and the exact formula for a portfolio's volatility that shows low correlation is the whole game. From there we combine assets and watch the risk–return curve **bend inward**, trace the **efficient frontier** and its minimum-variance tip, add a risk-free asset to draw the straight **Capital Market Line** that dominates the curve, and finish with **CAPM** — beta, the market risk premium, and the Security Market Line that prices systematic risk. By the end you won't just *believe* diversification helps; you'll be able to compute exactly how much, and say which portfolios are worth holding and which are dominated.
