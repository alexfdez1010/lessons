You have an edge. You have done the work — you know your win rate, your odds, your expected return. Now comes the question that decides whether that edge makes you rich or makes you broke: **how much do you bet?** Bet too little and your edge compounds at a crawl. Bet too much and the same edge — the *winning* edge — drags you to zero anyway, because volatility eats geometric growth alive. There is exactly one bet size that maximizes how fast your money compounds, and the math that finds it is one of the most beautiful and most abused results in all of finance: the **Kelly criterion**.

But Kelly only makes sense once you have internalized a deeper, stranger truth that trips up almost everyone — including people who should know better. **The average return is a liar.** A portfolio that returns +50% one year and −50% the next has an *arithmetic average* return of 0%, yet it has lost 25% of your money. What you actually earn over time is not the arithmetic mean of your returns; it is the **geometric mean**, and the geometric mean is always lower — dragged below the average by a penalty equal to roughly half your variance. This **volatility drag** is the silent tax on every volatile strategy, the reason CAGR sits below the average annual return, and the reason that maximizing your expected return and maximizing your *wealth* are two completely different problems.

This topic builds the whole edifice from that foundation. First, **arithmetic vs geometric mean** — why a gain and an equal loss leave you poorer, and why the geometric mean is the only average that compounds. Then **CAGR and volatility drag** — the variance penalty $g \approx \mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$, made visual, and what it means that two portfolios with identical average returns can compound at wildly different rates. With that in hand we derive **the Kelly criterion** — bet the fraction $f^* = p - q/b$ of your bankroll, the size that maximizes the *expected log* of your wealth, equivalently its long-run growth rate. We extend it to real markets with **continuous Kelly** ($f^* = \mu/\sigma^2$), and confront the brutal swings of full Kelly with **fractional Kelly** — why half-Kelly keeps three-quarters of the growth for half the volatility, and why almost every professional bets a fraction. Finally we go to **multiple assets and the edge of ruin** — vector Kelly across correlated bets, what leverage really costs, and the iron law that betting past *twice* Kelly drives your long-run growth negative: you go broke with probability one, edge or no edge.

This is where probability, compounding, and risk management fuse into a single discipline. By the end you will never again confuse a high average return with a fast-growing one, you will be able to size a position so your edge actually compounds, and you will understand — in your bones, not just your spreadsheet — why the graveyard of trading is full of people who were right about the market and wrong about the bet size.
