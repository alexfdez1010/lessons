You already know what a blockchain *is* — a shared, append-only ledger a crowd of strangers
keeps honest without a boss — and you've seen Bitcoin use one to make digital cash that
nobody can counterfeit or censor. **Ethereum** takes the same foundation and asks a bigger
question: what if the shared ledger could run *programs*, not just record payments?

That one idea turns a payment network into a **world computer** — a single machine that
thousands of nodes run in lockstep, where anyone can deploy code that then executes exactly
as written, with no company able to alter or switch it off. This topic opens it all the way
up, assuming nothing beyond crypto-basics.

We build it as a ladder. First **what Ethereum is and how it differs from Bitcoin** — why a
programmable chain needs a different design. Then the **EVM, the world computer** itself:
how a transaction becomes a deterministic change to a shared state. We contrast Ethereum's
**account model with Bitcoin's UTXO** bookkeeping, then follow the meter that makes it all
pay for itself — **gas and fees under EIP-1559**, where part of every fee is burned. Next the
headline act: **smart contracts and tokens**, where a "token" turns out to be nothing more
than a balance sheet inside a contract (ERC-20 for fungible coins, ERC-721 for NFTs). We'll
see how Ethereum secures itself with **proof-of-stake and staking** instead of mining, and
finish with **Layer-2 rollups** — how Ethereum scales to cheap transactions without giving up
the security of the base chain. Get these seven and Ethereum stops being a buzzword and
starts being a machine you understand.
