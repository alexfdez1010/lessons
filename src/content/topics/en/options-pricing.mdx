You already know what an option *is* and how to read its hockey-stick payoff. This topic answers the harder question: **what is the fair price to pay for it today?** A payoff diagram tells you what an option is worth *at expiry*, for every possible final price. But you have to pay the premium *now*, before you know which of those futures shows up. Turning "all the things that might happen" into a single number on a screen is the whole art of options pricing — and it rests on one beautiful, slightly mind-bending idea: you can price an option **without ever guessing where the stock is going**.

We build that machine from the ground up. First, **why a payoff isn't a price** and why naive "expected value" pricing fails — it would have you discount at a rate nobody can agree on. The fix is **no-arbitrage and replication**: if you can rebuild an option's payoff with a recipe of stock and cash, the option must cost exactly what the recipe costs, or free money appears. That recipe leads straight to **risk-neutral valuation** and the **binomial tree** — a model so simple you can price an option with a pen, yet rich enough that, sliced fine enough, it *becomes* Black–Scholes. Then the main event: the **Black–Scholes formula** itself — where it comes from, what its assumptions buy and cost, and the real meaning hiding inside `d₁` and `d₂` (spoiler: `N(d₂)` is a probability). From a price we differentiate to get the **Greeks** — delta, gamma, theta, vega and rho — the sensitivities that tell a trader how their position breathes as the world moves, and how to **hedge** it. Finally we run the formula *backwards* to extract **implied volatility**, discover that the market quotes a different vol at every strike — the **smile and the skew** — and see why that single curve quietly admits Black–Scholes is only ever an approximation.

This is the bridge from "I can read an option" to "I can price, hedge and trade one." It's the most quantitative course on the ladder so far — every section earns its formula with an analogy, a worked number, and a chart you can poke.
