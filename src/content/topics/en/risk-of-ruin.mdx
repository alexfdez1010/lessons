Kelly taught you the optimal *growth* bet, and Monte Carlo taught you to roll the dice on a strategy ten thousand times. Risk of ruin is where those two ideas collide with the one constraint neither fully spelled out: **you have to survive.** A strategy with a genuine edge and a beautiful long-run growth rate is still worthless to a trader who gets wiped out in month three — because compounding only rewards the accounts that are *still open*. This topic is the math of staying in the game: not "how fast can I grow?" but "how likely am I to blow up before the edge ever pays?"

Here is the uncomfortable truth that organizes the whole subject: **a positive expectancy does not protect you from ruin.** You can have a system that wins on average, every single trade, and still go broke with frightening probability — if you bet too large, get a normal-but-unlucky losing streak, or meet your worst returns at the worst possible time. Edge tells you the game is worth playing. *Risk of ruin tells you whether you'll still be at the table when the edge finally shows up.* Confusing the two is how skilled traders and well-modeled funds quietly destroy themselves, and untangling them is the entire point of what follows.

The ladder climbs in six rungs. We start with **what ruin actually is** — a precise, absorbing barrier (an account too small to keep trading), the classic *gambler's ruin* result that even a perfectly fair game bankrupts a finite purse, and the central paradox that a winning system can still ruin you. Then **expectancy and the edge**: the per-trade engine, expectancy = (average win × win rate) − (average loss × loss rate), the elegant **R-multiple** bookkeeping that normalizes every trade to a single unit of risk, and the trade-off between **win rate** and **payoff ratio** that lets a system win 35% of the time and still print money. With the edge defined, we derive the **classic risk-of-ruin formula** — how edge, odds, and the number of *units of risk* in your bankroll combine into a survival probability, why ruin falls *exponentially* in the units of capital you hold, and several fully worked examples.

From there we open up the shape of the pain. **Drawdown distributions** treats the worst peak-to-trough fall not as a single number but as a *random variable* — depth, duration, and the dreaded *time under water* all have distributions, and the same system run twice gives two very different worst-cases. **Sequencing risk** delivers the most counterintuitive result in the topic: with no cashflows, the *order* of your returns is irrelevant (multiplication commutes), but the instant you add withdrawals or contributions, order becomes destiny — the retiree who meets a crash in year one can run dry while an identical retiree who meets it in year fifteen sails through. Finally, **Monte Carlo ruin curves and stops** puts it all together with simulation: estimating ruin probability by brute force, plotting ruin against risk-per-trade, and the stop-loss arithmetic that turns a risk budget into an exact position size — the practical machinery every risk desk runs before sizing a single trade. A graded **final exam** closes the loop.

Master this and you'll have the survival half of the quant toolkit that Kelly and Monte Carlo only hinted at. You'll size positions so a bad month is a flesh wound, not a funeral; you'll read a drawdown distribution and a ruin curve the way a risk manager does; and you'll never again confuse "this system has an edge" with "this system is safe." Because in the end, the trader who survives the longest, not the one who bets the biggest, is the one who gets to compound.
