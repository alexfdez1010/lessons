In Monte Carlo you learned to manufacture tomorrow by rolling dice ten thousand times. But there was a quiet sleight of hand in every one of those simulations: *something* told the dice what to do. When you wrote a geometric Brownian motion price path, you typed a drift, a volatility, and a normal random shock — and trusted that this little recipe was a faithful model of how a stock actually wanders. Stochastic processes are where you stop trusting and start understanding. Monte Carlo rolls the dice; **stochastic processes are the rulebook for which dice, why those dice, and what guarantees they buy you.** This is the theory of the random engine itself.

A stochastic process is just a **random variable that unfolds in time** — a price, a rate, a position that takes a fresh draw at every tick and traces out a jagged path as it goes. That sounds simple, and the simple version (a coin-flip random walk) really is. But layer on a few honest questions — *Is the next step independent of the past? Is the game fair? Does the path remember where it's been? What happens when you let the steps get infinitely small and infinitely frequent?* — and you tumble straight into the deep machinery under modern finance: Brownian motion, Itô calculus, and the stochastic differential equations that the Black–Scholes formula, every interest-rate model, and every quant pricing library are quietly built on.

This topic climbs that ladder one rung at a time. We start with **what a stochastic process actually is** — a whole family of random variables indexed by time, the difference between a single sample *path* and the cross-sectional *distribution* of where you might be at one moment, the idea of a **filtration** (information that only ever grows as the story unfolds), and what it means for a process to be stationary or to have independent increments. Then **random walks and martingales**: the humble coin-flip walk, what *drift* adds, and the **martingale** — a fair game where your best guess for tomorrow is exactly today — which turns out to be the precise mathematical fingerprint of an efficient market. Next, **Markov chains** and the memoryless property: transition matrices, n-step transitions, stationary distributions, and how a market that flips between calm and crisis regimes is just a chain in disguise.

From there we cross into continuous time with **Brownian motion** — the scaling limit you get when a random walk takes infinitely many infinitesimal steps. Its increments are independent and Gaussian, its paths are continuous yet *nowhere differentiable* (you cannot draw a tangent to a stock chart, and that's not a flaw — it's the whole point), and its strange **quadratic variation** is the seed from which all of stochastic calculus grows. Then the crown jewel: **Itô's lemma and SDEs**. We unpack what a stochastic differential equation really says — a deterministic **drift** plus a random **diffusion** — why ordinary calculus breaks the instant you put it on a Brownian path, where the famous $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ correction term comes from, and why solving the SDE for a stock gives you **geometric Brownian motion** and lognormal prices. Finally, **mean reversion and jumps**: the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process that pulls rates and spreads back toward a long-run level (with a tidy *half-life* you can read straight off the parameters when $\kappa > 0$), and the **Merton jump-diffusion** that bolts sudden gaps onto smooth diffusion to capture fat tails and overnight crash risk — plus how to actually choose between these models for a real series. The topic closes with a **graded exam** to prove the whole structure stuck.

Master this and you've learned the native language of quantitative finance. Every option-pricing model, every term-structure model, every risk engine you meet from here on is written in stochastic differential equations — and once you can read the rulebook behind the dice, the rest of the field stops being magic and starts being grammar.
