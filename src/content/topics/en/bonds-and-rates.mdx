You already know that money has a clock and that lending it earns interest. A **bond**
takes that idea and makes it *tradable*: instead of lending to your cousin and waiting,
you lend to a government or a company, get a piece of paper promising fixed payments,
and — here's the twist — you can sell that paper to someone else tomorrow. That's the
whole magic. A bond is a loan with a resale market bolted on. And the instant a loan
becomes tradable, its price has to move, because the world's interest rates keep
changing while your bond's payments stay frozen. Rates run the entire game, and this
topic is about learning to play it.

This topic climbs the ladder one rung at a time, assuming you've met assets and yield
but never cracked open a bond. First, **what a bond actually is** — coupon, face value,
maturity, and the cast of characters on that piece of paper. Then the single most
important relationship in fixed income: **bond prices and yields**, the see-saw where
one goes up only when the other goes down. Next, the **yield curve** — what it means
when long-term rates sit above (or, ominously, below) short-term ones. Then we measure
how *much* a bond lurches when rates move: **duration**, the bond's effective
time-to-payback and its rate-sensitivity gauge — followed by **convexity**, the
correction that explains why duration alone always lies a little. We finish with a
**final exam** that makes you trade the see-saw for real. Get these six and a bond
prospectus stops looking like hieroglyphics.
