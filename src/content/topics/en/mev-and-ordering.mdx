Every transaction you send on a public blockchain sits, for a few seconds, in plain sight — broadcast to a **mempool** where anyone in the world can read it before it is confirmed. And whoever assembles the next block gets to decide not just *which* of those pending transactions go in, but **in what order**. On a chain where prices move with every trade and a swap's outcome depends on what came right before it, that ordering power is not a clerical detail. It is money. The amount of value you can squeeze out of a block purely by choosing what to include, exclude, and reorder has a name: **Maximal Extractable Value (MEV)**.

MEV is the dark matter of decentralized finance — invisible in the user interface, enormous in aggregate, and shaping the design of Ethereum itself. It is why your DEX swap sometimes fills at a suspiciously bad price, why arbitrage between pools is instantaneous, why a loan gets liquidated the same block it crosses the line, and why "just send the transaction" hides a competitive, automated, billion-dollar war over sequencing.

This topic builds the whole machine from the mempool up. First, **what MEV actually is** and why the right to order transactions has value at all. Then the **mempool and the ordering mechanism** — how transactions are picked, and how priority fees turned ordering into a live auction. From there, the attacks: **front-running and back-running** (getting in just ahead of or just behind a victim), and the signature DeFi predator — the **sandwich attack**, which we'll work out with exact constant-product math so you can compute the victim's loss and the attacker's profit yourself. Next, the *benign* (or at least load-bearing) side of MEV — **arbitrage and liquidations** — that keeps prices aligned and lending markets solvent. Finally, the countermeasures: **Proposer-Builder Separation, MEV-Boost, relays, private orderflow, fair ordering and encrypted mempools** — the rapidly evolving attempt to keep extraction from eating the chain.

This sits at the top of the crypto ladder: it assumes you already understand how Ethereum orders and prices transactions and how an AMM walks a curve. By the end, "I made a swap" stops being a single click and becomes a transparent, adversarial pipeline — one you can reason about, price, and defend against.
