You already know *what* money is — barter's cure, the three-function Swiss-army knife,
the leap from commodity to fiat. This course is the *story of how it got there*, and it
is wilder than any textbook lets on. Money is not a fixed thing handed down from on high;
it's a 5,000-year-old invention that humanity keeps **re-engineering** — usually under the
pressure of war, trade imbalances, runaway inflation, or collapsing trust.

We zoom in on the **20th century**, when money changed shape more violently than in all the
millennia before it. We start with the **gold standard** — the era when a pound or a dollar
was a fixed weight of metal, and why that golden chain both disciplined nations and, when
it snapped, dragged them into depression. Then the carnage: **World War I** prints money off
the gold leash, and 1923 **Weimar Germany** shows the whole world what happens when a currency
dies — wheelbarrows of cash for a loaf of bread.

Out of that wreckage comes the most ambitious monetary deal ever struck: **Bretton Woods (1944)**,
where 44 nations pegged their currencies to the dollar and the dollar to gold, and invented the
IMF and World Bank to police it. It held for a generation — until it couldn't. On a Sunday night
in **August 1971**, President Nixon went on television and quietly ended gold convertibility,
launching the **pure fiat era** and floating exchange rates we still live in today.

From there money goes **electronic** — the eurodollar market, wire transfers, credit cards, ATMs —
and then, after the **2008 financial crisis** shattered trust in banks, it goes one step further:
**code**. In January 2009 the Bitcoin genesis block is mined, and money-as-software is born. Five
lessons trace that whole arc — *why* each transformation happened and *what* was traded away —
then a capstone exam to prove the timeline stuck.
