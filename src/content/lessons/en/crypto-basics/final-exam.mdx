## Section 1 — What a Blockchain Is

The whole course rests on this: a blockchain is a shared, append-only ledger whose history is *tamper-evident*, not secret. If you can't say precisely how blocks link and why old history can't be quietly rewritten, nothing downstream makes sense.

## Section 2 — Keys & Wallets

The single biggest beginner trap lives here: a wallet does **not** hold your coins. It holds your *keys*. The coins live on the ledger; the keys are what let you move them. Get the public/private split and the seed phrase straight, or you'll lose money.

    { text: "Correct — the coins are downloaded into the wallet whenever you go online" },

  explanation="A wallet is a keyring, not a vault. Your balance is just entries on the shared ledger; the wallet holds the private key that authorizes spending them. Lose the app but keep the keys (seed phrase) and your funds are untouched."

## Section 3 — Transactions & Fees

What actually happens between hitting *send* and the money being safely yours? A signed transaction takes a journey, block space is scarce, and the fee is an auction — paid to the block producer, never the receiver.

<MCQ
  client:visible
  question="Put the life of a transaction in the right order."

    { text: "Broadcast → confirmed instantly → signed later by the recipient → archived in the mempool" },
    { text: "Sent to the exchange → exchange signs it → it appears in your wallet with zero confirmations needed" },
    { text: "Included in a block → then signed → then broadcast → then dropped into the mempool" },
  ]}
  explanation="You sign with your private key, broadcast it, it sits in the mempool (the waiting room of unconfirmed transactions), a block producer includes it, and then confirmations stack up. Signing comes first; confirmations come last."
/>

## Section 4 — UTXO vs Account

The grand fork in design. Bitcoin tracks discrete coins (UTXOs) you spend whole, getting change back. Ethereum tracks one running balance per account, ordered by a nonce. The classic trap: thinking "change" means you lost money, or that Bitcoin stores a balance number like a bank.

<MatchConcepts
  client:visible
  question="Match each term to its exact meaning in the two models."
  pairs={[
    { term: 'UTXO (Bitcoin)', definition: 'A discrete coin spent whole, with leftover value returned to you as change.' },
    { term: 'Bitcoin balance', definition: 'Not stored as a number — it is the SUM of all the UTXOs you control.' },
    { term: 'Change output', definition: 'The leftover value sent back to you when a UTXO is bigger than what you owe.' },
    { term: 'Account (Ethereum)', definition: 'A single running balance for your address, updated up and down like a bank account.' },
    { term: 'Nonce', definition: 'A per-account counter that orders an account’s transactions and blocks replays.' },
  ]}
  explanation="UTXO = discrete coin spent whole; your Bitcoin balance is the SUM of UTXOs, not a stored number. Change is your own leftover coming back. Ethereum keeps one running balance per account, with a nonce ordering its transactions."
/>

## You passed if…

<Callout variant="success" title="The rubric — did it stick?">
You've genuinely finished this course if you can, without peeking:

- **Say what a blockchain is** — an append-only, *tamper-evident* (not encrypted) ledger, with blocks linked by the previous block's hash, replicated across nodes, one shared chain chosen by consensus — and explain why "blockchain = Bitcoin" and "it's a central database" are both wrong.
- **Explain who owns a wallet** — it holds *keys*, not coins; the private key signs and stays secret; the address is safe to share; the seed phrase is the only recovery with no reset; and "not your keys, not your coins" separates self-custody from a custodial exchange.
- **Trace a transaction** — sign → broadcast → mempool → block → confirmations; know that block space is scarce, fees are an auction that pays the **block producer** (not the receiver), gas measures computation, and one confirmation isn't true finality.
- **Tell UTXO from account** — Bitcoin's discrete coins spent whole with change returned (balance = sum of UTXOs) versus Ethereum's single running balance ordered by a nonce — and recite the trade-offs without calling change a "loss."

Aced it? You now understand the machinery under every coin and every DeFi app — which is exactly what you need before going deeper.
