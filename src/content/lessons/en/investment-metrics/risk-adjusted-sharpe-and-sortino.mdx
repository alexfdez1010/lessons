By now you can measure how *much* an investment grew (ROI, CAGR) and how *scary*
the ride was (volatility, drawdown). But those two halves only matter **together**.
A 60% gain on a serene ride is a triumph; the same 60% on a stomach-churning
rollercoaster is mostly luck. The metrics in this lesson fuse return and risk into a
single number — so you can finally answer the question that actually decides
quality: *was the return worth the risk?*

<Callout variant="info" title="The question this lesson answers">
- **Was the return worth the risk?** → Sharpe ratio, Sortino ratio, Calmar ratio

All three divide *return* by *risk*. Sharpe and Sortino differ only in which
flavor of *volatility* lands in the denominator; Calmar swaps in a completely
different kind of return **and** risk — more on that below.
</Callout>

## Sharpe Ratio: Return Per Unit of Risk

Return and risk are only meaningful **together**. Watch two portfolios finish in the
exact same place by completely different roads — same destination, wildly different
journey:

The **Sharpe ratio** captures this in one number: return earned *above a risk-free
baseline*, divided by volatility.

$$\text{Sharpe} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}$$

where $R_p$ is the portfolio return, $R_f$ the risk-free rate (roughly a government
T-bill), and $\sigma_p$ the volatility — the standard deviation of returns you met
in the risk lesson.

### Worked example

A fund returns **12%**, the risk-free rate is **2%**, and volatility is **15%**:

$$\text{Sharpe} = \frac{12\% - 2\%}{15\%} = \frac{10}{15} \approx 0.67$$

### Reading the number

| Sharpe ratio | Rough verdict |
|---|---|
| < 0 | You'd have done better in T-bills |
| 0 – 1 | Acceptable to mediocre |
| 1 – 2 | Good |
| 2 – 3 | Very good |
| > 3 | Excellent (or too good to be true) |

<Callout variant="tip" title="Why subtract the risk-free rate?">
You should only get credit for returns *above* what risk-free cash would have paid.
If T-bills yield 2% and your fund made 2%, your risk earned you nothing — and the
numerator correctly collapses to zero.
</Callout>

## Sortino Ratio: Punishing Only the Bad Volatility

    { text: "Uses drawdown instead of return", correct: false },
    { text: "Adds beta to the numerator", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Sortino divides excess return by DOWNSIDE deviation only. Big upside surprises don't count as 'risk' — many investors find that fairer."

The Sharpe ratio has one quirk: it penalizes **all** volatility, including the
upside. But nobody complains about a +20% month. The **Sortino ratio** fixes this by
dividing only by **downside deviation** — the volatility of returns that fell below
your target (often zero or the risk-free rate).

$$\text{Sortino} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_{\text{downside}}}$$

### Worked example

Same fund: excess return is **10%** (12% − 2%). Suppose its *downside* deviation is
only **8%** (because most of its volatility came from good months):

$$\text{Sortino} = \frac{10\%}{8\%} = 1.25$$

Notice it's **higher than the Sharpe of 0.67** — because we stopped punishing the
fund for its pleasant surprises. A big gap between Sortino and Sharpe tells you a
fund's volatility is mostly *upside* (a nice problem to have).

### When to use which

- **Sharpe** — the universal default; good for comparing anything, and what most
  factsheets quote.
- **Sortino** — better when a strategy has lopsided returns (lots of small wins, the
  occasional loss), e.g. options-selling or trend-following.

## Calmar Ratio: Return Per Unit of Pain

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: the Calmar ratio measures return against…"

    { text: "The standard deviation of all monthly returns", correct: false },
    { text: "Only the downside deviation below a target", correct: false },
    { text: "The fund's management fee", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Calmar divides growth by the worst drawdown — the deepest peak-to-trough fall. It answers: how much did you earn for the maximum pain you had to stomach?"
/>

Sharpe and Sortino both measure risk as *wiggle* — the standard deviation of
returns. The **Calmar ratio** asks a blunter, more visceral question: for the
**worst loss you ever had to sit through**, how much growth did you get?

$$\text{Calmar} = \frac{\text{CAGR}}{\lvert \text{Max Drawdown} \rvert}$$

This is where the means part ways. Notice the numerator is **CAGR — the
compound (geometric) annual growth rate**, not the arithmetic average of
periodic returns that Sharpe and Sortino use. Compounding *experiences* the
drawdowns, so pairing geometric growth with the worst drawdown keeps the whole
ratio grounded in what an investor actually lived through.

<Callout variant="info" title="Arithmetic vs geometric — the one that trips people up">
- **Sharpe & Sortino** put the **arithmetic** mean of excess returns on top.
- **Calmar** puts **CAGR** — the **geometric** mean — on top.

So if you ever heard "one of these uses the geometric mean," it's **Calmar**,
not Sortino. Geometric growth is the rate that, compounded each period,
reproduces your actual ending wealth; it's always ≤ the arithmetic mean
whenever returns vary.
</Callout>

### Worked example

A fund compounds at a **CAGR of 12%** and, over the same window, its deepest
peak-to-trough fall was a **max drawdown of 20%**:

$$\text{Calmar} = \frac{12\%}{20\%} = 0.6$$

A Calmar of **0.6** says: for every unit of *worst-case pain*, you earned 0.6
units of compound growth. Calmar is usually measured over a **3-year** window,
and a reading **above 1.0** is considered strong — your annual growth outran
your worst fall.

### When to use which

- **Sharpe** — the universal default; penalizes every swing.
- **Sortino** — when upside swings shouldn't count as "risk."
- **Calmar** — when the **worst-case loss** is what would actually scare you out
  of a position. Popular for hedge funds and trend-following CTAs, where
  surviving the deepest drawdown matters more than smoothing month-to-month
  wiggle.

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to remember">
- **Read return and risk together** — a big gain on a brutal ride is luck; a solid return on a calm ride is quality.
- **Sharpe** = excess return ($R_p - R_f$) ÷ *total* volatility. Higher is better; subtracting the risk-free rate credits only returns above safe cash.
- **Sortino** keeps the same numerator but divides only by *downside* deviation, so it stops punishing upside swings.
- A **Sortino much higher than Sharpe** means a fund's volatility is mostly the good kind.
- **Calmar** = **CAGR** ÷ *worst max drawdown* — the one ratio with a **geometric** (compound) numerator, not arithmetic. Above 1.0 over a 3-year window is strong.
- The "geometric mean" ratio is **Calmar**, not Sortino — Sharpe and Sortino both use the arithmetic mean of excess returns.
- Use **Sharpe** as the default; reach for **Sortino** when returns are lopsided, and **Calmar** when surviving the worst drawdown is the real concern.
</Callout>
