## Section 1 — Returns Under Pressure

A founder waves a chart at you: "We **tripled** the money!" Tripling is an ROI of
+200% — genuinely large. But three different funds tripled over three very different
horizons:

| Fund | Starting value | Ending value | Years held |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sprinter | \$10,000 | \$30,000 | 2 |
| Cruiser | \$10,000 | \$30,000 | 8 |
| Glacier | \$10,000 | \$30,000 | 20 |

All three share the identical ROI of +200%. But CAGR = (End / Start)^(1/years) − 1
spreads that gain across the clock. Tripling means End/Start = 3, so the only thing
that changes is the exponent: 3^(1/2) − 1 ≈ 73%/yr for Sprinter, 3^(1/8) − 1 ≈ 15%/yr
for Cruiser, and 3^(1/20) − 1 ≈ 5.6%/yr for Glacier. Same headline, wildly different
quality.

Now the subtler trap — the one that separates the arithmetic average from the truth.
A fund posts these four yearly returns: **+80%, −40%, +80%, −40%**.

The naive pitch: "Average return of +20% a year!" (because (80 − 40 + 80 − 40)/4 = 20).
But compounding *experiences* every loss. Multiply the growth factors:
1.80 × 0.60 × 1.80 × 0.60 = 1.1664 — so \$1 becomes about \$1.17 over four years. The
honest CAGR is 1.1664^(1/4) − 1 ≈ **3.9%/yr**, not 20%. The swings dragged compound
growth far below the simple average.

## Section 2 — Reading Risk

Two funds finished the decade at the same place, but the rides were nothing alike.
Here are their numbers:

| Metric | Fund Hummingbird | Fund Avalanche |
|---|---|---|
| CAGR | 9% | 9% |
| Annual volatility | 8% | 26% |
| Max drawdown | −15% | −68% |

Identical growth, but Avalanche's worst peak-to-trough fall was **−68%**. Recovery is
asymmetric — the gain needed to climb back is 1/(1 − d) − 1. Hummingbird's −15% needs
about 1/0.85 − 1 ≈ +18% to recover. Avalanche's −68% needs 1/0.32 − 1 ≈ **+213%** just
to get back to even. The same destination hid a near-death experience.

A common trap is assuming a low-volatility fund can't have a scary drawdown. Picture
a fund that drifts quietly upward for years — low monthly volatility — then takes one
single 55% plunge in a crash before recovering. Its volatility number can still look
tame because most months were calm, yet a 55% drawdown needs about +122% to recover.

## Section 3 — Was It Worth It?

Now fuse return and risk. Three funds posted the **same excess return** over the
risk-free rate, but the risk in the denominator differs by flavor:

| Fund | Excess return | Total volatility | Downside deviation | Max drawdown |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Metronome | 10% | 20% | 14% | −25% |
| Seesaw | 10% | 20% | 8% | −25% |
| Cliffside | 10% | 20% | 14% | −50% |

All three share a Sharpe of 10/20 = 0.5. But Seesaw's downside deviation is only 8%,
so its Sortino is 10/8 = 1.25 — far above its Sharpe, meaning most of Seesaw's
volatility was the *good* kind (upside). Cliffside has the same Sharpe and Sortino as
Metronome, but double the drawdown, so its Calmar (CAGR ÷ |max drawdown|) is the worst
of the three.

and Sortino, just over a longer window." That's the classic mix-up. Sharpe and Sortino
put the **arithmetic** mean of excess returns on top; Calmar is the odd one out — its
numerator is **CAGR**, the geometric (compound) growth rate, and it has no risk-free
subtraction at all.

## Section 4 — Skill or the Tide?

Two managers brag about the same 14% return in a roaring market. The risk-free rate
was 2% and the market (Rm) returned 12%. Their betas differ:

| Fund | Beta | Actual return | CAPM-expected return |
|---|---|---|---|
| Steady Hand | 0.8 | 14% | 2% + 0.8 × (12% − 2%) = 10% |
| Leverage Larry | 1.6 | 14% | 2% + 1.6 × (12% − 2%) = 18% |

CAPM expected return = Rf + β(Rm − Rf), and alpha = actual − expected. Steady Hand was
*expected* to earn 10% for its modest market exposure and delivered 14% — alpha of
**+4%**, genuine skill. Leverage Larry took so much market risk it *should* have made
18%, but delivered only 14% — alpha of **−4%**. Same headline return; one added value,
the other destroyed it while a rising tide flattered the number.

Now a beta-direction trap. A gold-heavy fund has a beta of **−0.5**. The market
*falls* 10% in a panic. Beta is the slope: a negative beta moves opposite the market,
so the model predicts −0.5 × (−10%) = **+5%** for the fund. Negative beta inverts the
market's move.

## Section 5 — The Full Gauntlet

The centerpiece. Here is one last factsheet pitting a quiet workhorse against a flashy
headline-grabber. Read it the way the four questions demand — how much, how risky,
worth it, skill or tide — before you answer the quiz below.

| Metric | Fund Tortoise | Fund Hare |
|---|---|---|
| CAGR | 10% | 16% |
| Volatility | 11% | 34% |
| Max drawdown | −16% | −64% |

| Sortino | 1.8 | 0.5 |
| Calmar | 0.6 | 0.25 |
| Beta | 0.9 | 1.9 |
| Alpha | +2.8% | −1.5% |

The Hare wins the headline (16% vs 10%) — and loses everything that matters. It ran
**triple** the volatility, fell **−64%** (needing roughly +178% to recover, versus the
Tortoise's −16% needing about +19%), earns less than half the return per unit of risk
on every ratio, leaned on a **1.9 beta** to let a roaring market carry it, and *still*
posted **negative alpha** — lagging what all that risk should have paid. The Tortoise
quietly wins on every measure of quality.

<Callout variant="success" title="You passed if…">
- You read **ROI and CAGR** as different jobs — total gain vs. the geometric yearly rate — and never let an arithmetic average disguise the volatility drag.
- You treat **volatility and drawdown** as complementary, and you instinctively reach for `1/(1 − d) − 1` when someone shrugs off a deep fall.
- You can keep **Sharpe, Sortino and Calmar** straight: two arithmetic ratios differing in the denominator, plus Calmar — the geometric, drawdown-based odd one out.
- You separate the **tide from the rowing**: beta is the ride, alpha is the skill, and a fat return on a high beta with negative alpha is luck (or leverage) wearing a costume.
- Above all, you judge a fund by reading the numbers **together**, and you can say the one line that summarizes the whole course: *a big return on a brutal ride is luck; a solid return on a calm ride is quality.*

That's the gauntlet. If those reflexes are automatic now, you can read a real factsheet
without getting played.
</Callout>
