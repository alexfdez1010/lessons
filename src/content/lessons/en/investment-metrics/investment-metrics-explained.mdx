A price chart that goes up feels like a win. But "it went up" is the *least*
interesting thing you can say about an investment. Did it grow faster than a
savings account? Did it keep you awake at night? Did the fund manager earn their
fee, or just get carried along by a rising market?

Each of those questions has a number that answers it. This guide walks through the
**eight metrics** that matter most — one section each, every one with a worked
example you can follow on paper and a chart you can poke at. We go in the order a
careful investor actually thinks: **how much** (return), **how scary** (risk),
**was it worth it** (risk-adjusted), and **was it skill** (market-relative).

<Callout variant="info" title="The four questions, eight numbers">
- **How much did it grow?** → ROI, CAGR
- **How risky was the ride?** → Volatility, Max drawdown
- **Was the return worth the risk?** → Sharpe, Sortino
- **Skill or just the market?** → Beta, Alpha
</Callout>

## ROI: The Honest but Forgetful Number

/>

**Return on Investment (ROI)** is the most basic measure: how much you made
relative to what you put in.

$$\text{ROI} = \frac{\text{final value} - \text{initial value}}{\text{initial value}}$$

### Worked example

You invest **\$80**, sell for **\$100**, and collected **\$4** in dividends along the
way. Total proceeds are \$104.

$$\text{ROI} = \frac{104 - 80}{80} = \frac{24}{80} = 0.30 = 30\%$$

<Callout variant="tip" title="Always include cash flows">
ROI should count *everything* you received — dividends, interest, coupons — not
just the price change. Leaving out the \$4 dividend understates the return at 25%
instead of 30%.
</Callout>

### The catch: ROI has no clock

ROI tells you the size of the gain but **nothing about how long it took**. A 30%
return is spectacular in one year and forgettable over ten. Watch how the same ROI
means completely different things depending on the holding period:

| Investment | ROI | Years held | Roughly per year |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fund A | 30% | 1 | ~30% |
| Fund B | 30% | 5 | ~5.4% |
| Fund C | 30% | 10 | ~2.7% |

Same headline number, three very different investments. To compare them fairly, we
need to put everything on a **per-year** footing — which is exactly what CAGR does.

## CAGR: Growth on a Per-Year Footing

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: an investment doubles (×2) over 10 years. Its compound annual growth rate is closest to…"
  options={[
    { text: "About 7% per year", correct: true },
    { text: "10% per year", correct: false },
    { text: "100% per year", correct: false },
    { text: "20% per year", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="(2)^(1/10) − 1 ≈ 7.2%. The naive 'doubled, so 100%/10 = 10%' ignores compounding — each year grows on top of the last, so a lower rate gets you there."
/>

The **Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR)** is the single steady yearly rate that
would take you from your starting value to your ending value, as if it grew the
same amount every year.

$$\text{CAGR} = \left(\frac{\text{End}}{\text{Start}}\right)^{1/\text{years}} - 1$$

The real path zig-zags; CAGR is the **one smooth curve** that connects the first
dot to the last. Drag the rate and the horizon below and watch compounding bend the
line away from naive straight-line growth:

<CompoundingCurve client:visible />

### Worked example

\$1,000 grows to \$2,000 over 10 years:

$$\text{CAGR} = \left(\frac{2000}{1000}\right)^{1/10} - 1 = 2^{0.1} - 1 \approx 1.0718 - 1 = 7.18\%$$

Not 100%, not 10% — about **7.2% per year**. Compounding does the heavy lifting: at
7.18%, each year's gain is computed on a bigger and bigger base.

### CAGR ≠ average return

Here's a subtlety that trips people up. Suppose a fund returns **+50%** one year
then **−50%** the next. The *average* return looks like 0%. The *actual* result?

$$ 1.50 \times 0.50 = 0.75 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{you lost } 25\% $$

The CAGR over those two years is `(0.75)^(1/2) − 1 ≈ −13.4%` per year. **Volatility
drags compound growth below the simple average** — which is why CAGR (a *geometric*
mean) is the honest growth number, not the arithmetic average.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The volatility drag">
The arithmetic average return always overstates what you actually earned when
returns bounce around. A +50% / −50% pair averages 0% but leaves you down 25%. The
bigger the swings, the bigger the gap.
</Callout>

## Volatility: The Bumpiness of the Ride

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: two funds both average 8% a year. What makes one 'riskier' by the standard measure?"
  options={[
    { text: "Its yearly returns swing further from that 8% average", correct: true },
    { text: "It has a higher 8%… they're the same number", correct: false },
    { text: "It pays fewer dividends", correct: false },
    { text: "It was launched more recently", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Volatility is the standard deviation of returns — how far they typically stray from the average, in either direction. Same average, wider spread = more volatile."
/>

Return tells you the destination; **risk** describes the road. The headline risk
number is **volatility** — the *standard deviation* of returns. Two funds can share
the same average yet feel completely different. Here are two return distributions
with an identical mean but very different spread — drag the slider to crank the risk
up and down:

<ReturnDistribution client:visible />

### Worked example: computing standard deviation

Take four yearly returns: **4%, 8%, 6%, 2%**.

1. **Mean:** (4 + 8 + 6 + 2) / 4 = **5%**
2. **Deviations from mean:** −1, +3, +1, −3
3. **Square them:** 1, 9, 1, 9 → sum = 20
4. **Variance:** 20 / 4 = 5
5. **Standard deviation:** √5 ≈ **2.24%**

So this fund's returns typically sit within about ±2.24% of their 5% average.

<Callout variant="info" title="Annualizing volatility">
Volatility is often measured from monthly data, then scaled up by the square root
of time: **annual σ ≈ monthly σ × √12**. A 4% monthly standard deviation is roughly
`4% × 3.46 ≈ 13.9%` annualized. (The √ comes from variance adding linearly over
independent periods.)
</Callout>

### The blind spot

Volatility treats a +15% surprise exactly like a −15% one — both are "deviation."
But you don't lie awake over gains. That asymmetry is why volatility alone is
incomplete, and why we also measure **drawdown** (next) and later split risk into
upside vs. downside (Sortino).

## Max Drawdown: The Pain You'd Have Lived Through

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: a fund climbs to \$200, falls to \$120, then recovers. What's its max drawdown?"
  options={[
    { text: "40%", correct: true },
    { text: "80%", correct: false },
    { text: "67%", correct: false },
    { text: "\$80", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Drawdown is measured from the PEAK: (200 − 120) / 200 = 80/200 = 40%. It's the deepest hole relative to the high-water mark."
/>

Volatility is the *typical* wobble. **Maximum drawdown** is the *worst* of it — the
largest peak-to-trough fall before a new high. It's the loss you'd actually have
endured at the worst possible moment, and the number that makes people panic-sell at
the bottom. Press play to watch a curve run up, crash, and have its deepest fall
measured automatically:

<DrawdownChart client:visible />

### Worked example

A fund peaks at **\$200**, bottoms at **\$120**, then recovers:

$$\text{Max drawdown} = \frac{200 - 120}{200} = \frac{80}{200} = 40\%$$

### Why drawdowns hurt twice: the recovery math

A drawdown is worse than it looks, because the gain needed to *recover* is bigger
than the loss. Losing 50% doesn't need +50% back — it needs **+100%**:

| Drawdown | Gain needed to recover |
|---|---|
| −10% | +11% |
| −20% | +25% |
| −33% | +50% |
| −50% | +100% |
| −80% | +400% |

The formula is `recovery = 1 / (1 − drawdown) − 1`. This asymmetry is the whole
reason "don't lose big" beats "win big" for long-run compounding.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Read volatility AND drawdown together">
A fund can look calm on average (low volatility) yet still have suffered one brutal
60% crash in a bad year. Volatility describes the *typical* month; drawdown exposes
the *worst* stretch. Neither alone is the full story.
</Callout>

## Sharpe Ratio: Return Per Unit of Risk

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: two funds both returned +60%. What would make one clearly better?"
  options={[
    { text: "The one that took less risk to earn the same 60%", correct: true },
    { text: "The one with the longer name", correct: false },
    { text: "They're identical — same return, same quality", correct: false },
    { text: "The one with the higher share price", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Same return, less risk = better deal. A risk-adjusted ratio rewards return PER unit of risk, not raw return."
/>

Return and risk are only meaningful **together**. A 60% gain on a serene ride is a
triumph; the same 60% on a stomach-churning rollercoaster is luck. Watch two
portfolios finish in the exact same place by completely different roads:

<RiskReturnRace client:visible />

The **Sharpe ratio** captures this in one number: return earned *above a risk-free
baseline*, divided by volatility.

$$\text{Sharpe} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}$$

where $R_p$ is the portfolio return, $R_f$ the risk-free rate (roughly a government
T-bill), and $\sigma_p$ the volatility.

### Worked example

A fund returns **12%**, the risk-free rate is **2%**, and volatility is **15%**:

$$\text{Sharpe} = \frac{12\% - 2\%}{15\%} = \frac{10}{15} \approx 0.67$$

### Reading the number

| Sharpe ratio | Rough verdict |
|---|---|
| < 0 | You'd have done better in T-bills |
| 0 – 1 | Acceptable to mediocre |
| 1 – 2 | Good |
| 2 – 3 | Very good |
| > 3 | Excellent (or too good to be true) |

<Callout variant="tip" title="Why subtract the risk-free rate?">
You should only get credit for returns *above* what risk-free cash would have paid.
If T-bills yield 2% and your fund made 2%, your risk earned you nothing — and the
numerator correctly collapses to zero.
</Callout>

## Sortino Ratio: Punishing Only the Bad Volatility

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: the Sortino ratio differs from Sharpe mainly because it…"
  options={[
    { text: "Only counts downside volatility, ignoring upside swings", correct: true },
    { text: "Removes the risk-free rate from the formula", correct: false },
    { text: "Uses drawdown instead of return", correct: false },
    { text: "Adds beta to the numerator", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Sortino divides excess return by DOWNSIDE deviation only. Big upside surprises don't count as 'risk' — many investors find that fairer."
/>

The Sharpe ratio has one quirk: it penalizes **all** volatility, including the
upside. But nobody complains about a +20% month. The **Sortino ratio** fixes this by
dividing only by **downside deviation** — the volatility of returns that fell below
your target (often zero or the risk-free rate).

$$\text{Sortino} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_{\text{downside}}}$$

### Worked example

Same fund: excess return is **10%** (12% − 2%). Suppose its *downside* deviation is
only **8%** (because most of its volatility came from good months):

$$\text{Sortino} = \frac{10\%}{8\%} = 1.25$$

Notice it's **higher than the Sharpe of 0.67** — because we stopped punishing the
fund for its pleasant surprises. A big gap between Sortino and Sharpe tells you a
fund's volatility is mostly *upside* (a nice problem to have).

### When to use which

- **Sharpe** — the universal default; good for comparing anything, and what most
  factsheets quote.
- **Sortino** — better when a strategy has lopsided returns (lots of small wins, the
  occasional loss), e.g. options-selling or trend-following.

## Beta: How Much You Move With the Market

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: a stock has a beta of 2. The market drops 10% in a day. What does theory expect for the stock?"
  options={[
    { text: "About −20%", correct: true },
    { text: "About −10%", correct: false },
    { text: "About −5%", correct: false },
    { text: "About +20%", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Beta is the amplification factor. β = 2 means the stock tends to move twice as much as the market: a −10% market day implies roughly −20% for the stock."
/>

**Beta** measures how strongly a portfolio moves *with the market*. It's the slope
of your returns plotted against the market's returns. Toggle the presets below to
see a defensive, market-tracking, and aggressive portfolio — and notice the line's
**slope** is beta and its **height at zero** is alpha:

<BetaScatter client:visible />

| Beta | Meaning |
|---|---|
| β = 1 | Moves in lockstep with the market |
| β > 1 | Amplifies the market (more aggressive, more risk) |
| 0 < β < 1 | Dampens the market (more defensive) |
| β ≈ 0 | Barely reacts to the market (e.g. cash, some hedge funds) |
| β < 0 | Moves *opposite* the market (e.g. gold in some regimes) |

### Worked example

Your portfolio has a beta of **1.3**. The market rises **10%**. The
market-explained part of your return is `1.3 × 10% = 13%`. If the market had instead
fallen 10%, beta would predict −13% — beta cuts both ways.

<Callout variant="info" title="Beta is about co-movement, not quality">
A high beta isn't 'good' or 'bad' — it just means more market exposure. In a bull
market high beta flatters you; in a crash it punishes you. Beta tells you *how much
market risk* you're carrying, nothing about skill. For skill, you need alpha.
</Callout>

## Alpha: The Part That's Actually Skill

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: a fund returned +25% last year. The market also returned +25%. How much skill did the manager likely add?"
  options={[
    { text: "Roughly none — it just matched the market", correct: true },
    { text: "A lot — 25% is a big number", correct: false },
    { text: "Impossible to tell from returns at all", correct: false },
    { text: "Negative — matching the market is failure", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Matching a +25% market with +25% is a rising tide lifting all boats — that's beta, not alpha. Skill shows up as return ABOVE what your market exposure predicted."
/>

**Alpha** is the return a portfolio earned *beyond what its beta predicted* — the
manager's genuine value-add (or destruction) after accounting for the market ride
they took. The baseline comes from the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM):

$$\text{Expected return} = R_f + \beta \,(R_m - R_f)$$
$$\alpha = \text{actual return} - \text{expected return}$$

### Worked example

Risk-free rate **2%**, your beta **1.2**, the market returned **9%**. CAPM expects:

$$R_f + \beta(R_m - R_f) = 2\% + 1.2 \times (9\% - 2\%) = 2\% + 1.2 \times 7\% = 10.4\%$$

If your fund actually returned **13%**, then:

$$\alpha = 13\% - 10.4\% = +2.6\%$$

That **+2.6%** is the skill — return the market exposure alone can't explain. A
*negative* alpha means a rising market flattered a strategy that actually
underperformed its risk.

## Putting It All Together: Read a Factsheet

Real analysis means reading the metrics *together*. Here are two funds that both
posted a great-looking CAGR — but the full picture tells very different stories:

| Metric | Fund A "Steady" | Fund B "Cowboy" |
|---|---|---|
| CAGR | 11% | 14% |
| Volatility | 10% | 32% |
| Max drawdown | −18% | −61% |
| Sharpe | 1.4 | 0.5 |
| Sortino | 1.9 | 0.6 |
| Beta | 0.9 | 1.8 |
| Alpha | +2.5% | −1.0% |

Fund B has the bigger CAGR, but it took *triple* the volatility, suffered a
near-fatal 61% drawdown, has a poor Sharpe/Sortino, and its **negative alpha** says
the manager actually *lagged* what that much market risk should have earned. Fund A
quietly wins on every risk-adjusted measure. **A big return on a brutal ride is
luck; a solid return on a calm ride is quality.**

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to remember">
- **ROI** is total gain; **CAGR** turns it into a steady, compounding-aware yearly rate (and sits *below* the simple average when returns swing).
- **Volatility** is the typical bumpiness; **max drawdown** is the worst fall — and recovering from a drawdown needs a *bigger* gain than the loss.
- **Sharpe** = excess return per unit of *total* risk; **Sortino** only counts *downside* risk, so it rewards mostly-upside strategies.
- **Beta** is how much you move with the market; **alpha** is the return beta can't explain — the genuine skill.
- Always read **return and risk together**: a big gain on a brutal ride is luck, not quality.
</Callout>
