A price chart that goes up feels like a win. But "it went up" is the *least*
interesting thing you can say about an investment. Did it grow faster than a
savings account? Did it keep you awake at night? Did the fund manager earn their
fee, or just get carried along by a rising market? Each of those questions has a
number that answers it — and once you know the handful that matter, you can read
any fund factsheet like a local.

## How Much Did It Grow? ROI and CAGR

<MCQ

  pretest

  explanation="Same total gain, wildly different speed. Doubling in 2 years is roughly 41%/year; doubling in 10 is about 7%/year. That gap is exactly why we need an annualized rate."
/>

The simplest measure is **Return on Investment (ROI)**: how much you made relative
to what you put in.

<Callout variant="info" title="ROI in one line">
**ROI = (final value − initial value) ÷ initial value.** Turn $1,000 into $1,300
and your ROI is $300 ÷ $1,000 = **30%**.
</Callout>

ROI is honest but forgetful: it has **no sense of time**. A 30% return is great
over one year and mediocre over ten. To compare investments held for different
lengths of time, we annualize with **CAGR — the Compound Annual Growth Rate**:
the single steady yearly rate that would take you from start to finish.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The smoothing analogy">
CAGR is the "if it had grown the same every year" rate. The real path may zig-zag,
but CAGR draws the one smooth exponential curve that connects the first dot to the
last. **CAGR = (End ÷ Start)^(1 ÷ years) − 1.**
</Callout>

So $1,000 → $2,000 over 10 years is `(2)^(1/10) − 1 ≈ 7.2%` a year — not 100%, and
not 10%. Compounding does the heavy lifting.

## How Wild Was the Ride? Volatility and Drawdown

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: 'volatility' of a portfolio mostly measures…"
  options={[
    { text: "How much its returns bounce around the average", correct: true },
    { text: "How much total money it has lost", correct: false },
    { text: "How often it beats the stock market", correct: false },
    { text: "How long you've held it", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Volatility is the spread of returns — how far they swing from the average — measured by standard deviation. Big swings (up or down) mean high volatility."
/>

Return tells you the destination. **Risk** tells you about the road. The headline
risk number is **volatility** — the *standard deviation* of returns. It measures
how far returns typically stray from their average, in either direction.

<Callout variant="info" title="Volatility, intuitively">
Two funds average 8% a year. One delivers 7%, 9%, 8%, 8% — boringly tight. The
other delivers −15%, +35%, −10%, +22% — same average, white-knuckle ride. The
second has far higher **volatility**.
</Callout>

Volatility treats up-swings and down-swings the same. But what actually hurts is
the **maximum drawdown** — the worst peak-to-trough fall before a new high.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Drawdown is the pain metric">
**Max drawdown** = the largest drop from a peak to a later trough. A fund that
climbs to $200 then sinks to $120 before recovering had a max drawdown of
(200 − 120) ÷ 200 = **40%**. It's the loss you'd have lived through at the worst
moment — and the one that makes people sell at the bottom.
</Callout>

<Reveal client:visible>
<Callout variant="tip" title="Why both matter">
Volatility tells you how bumpy the *typical* month is; drawdown tells you how bad
the *worst* stretch got. A fund can look calm on average yet still have had one
brutal 50% crash — so always read them together.
</Callout>

## Was the Return Worth the Risk? Sharpe and Sortino

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: two funds both returned +60%. What would make one clearly better than the other?"
  options={[
    { text: "The one that took less risk to earn the same 60%", correct: true },
    { text: "The one with the longer track record only", correct: false },
    { text: "They're identical — same return, same quality", correct: false },
    { text: "The one with the higher share price", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Identical returns, different risk → the calmer fund is the better deal. That's the whole point of a risk-adjusted ratio: reward return *per unit of risk*."
/>

Here's the trap: return and risk are only meaningful **together**. A 60% gain on a
serene ride is a triumph; the same 60% on a stomach-churning rollercoaster is luck.
Watch two portfolios finish in the exact same place by very different roads:

<RiskReturnRace client:visible />

The fix is a **risk-adjusted return**. The famous one is the **Sharpe ratio**:
return earned *above a risk-free baseline*, divided by volatility.

<Callout variant="info" title="Sharpe ratio">
**Sharpe = (portfolio return − risk-free rate) ÷ volatility.** It answers: *how
much extra return did I get for each unit of risk I took?* Higher is better. A
Sharpe around 1 is solid; above 2 is excellent. The "risk-free rate" is what you'd
earn doing nothing risky — roughly a government T-bill.
</Callout>

The Sharpe ratio has one quirk: it penalizes **all** volatility, including the
upside. But nobody complains about a portfolio that jumps +20%. The **Sortino
ratio** fixes this by dividing only by **downside** deviation — the volatility of
losses.

## Skill or a Rising Tide? Alpha and Beta

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess: a fund returned +25% last year. The market returned +25% too. How much skill did the manager likely add?"
  options={[
    { text: "Roughly none — it just matched the market", correct: true },
    { text: "A lot — 25% is a big number", correct: false },
    { text: "Impossible to tell from returns at all", correct: false },
    { text: "Negative — matching the market is failure", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Beating the market is the bar for skill. Matching a +25% market with a +25% return is a tide lifting all boats — that's beta, not alpha."
/>

The last pair separates **luck from skill**. **Beta** measures how much a
portfolio moves *with the market*. A beta of 1 means it swings exactly like the
market; 1.5 means it amplifies moves by 50%; 0.5 means it's half as jumpy.

<Callout variant="info" title="Beta = market sensitivity">
**Beta** is the slope of your returns against the market's. β = 1 tracks the
market, β > 1 is more aggressive, β < 1 is more defensive, and β near 0 barely
reacts to the market at all.
</Callout>

**Alpha** is the part of the return that beta *can't* explain — the manager's
genuine value-add (or destruction) after accounting for the market ride they took.

<Callout variant="success" title="Alpha = skill, after adjusting for risk">
**Alpha** is the excess return above what the portfolio's beta predicted. Positive
alpha = beat expectations; negative alpha = a rising market flattered a weak
strategy. It's the number that asks: *did you actually add value, or just hold a
risky basket while the market rose?*
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to remember">
- **ROI** is total gain; **CAGR** turns it into a steady yearly rate that respects compounding.
- **Volatility** is the bumpiness of the ride; **max drawdown** is the worst fall from a peak.
- **Sharpe** = excess return per unit of *total* risk; **Sortino** only counts *downside* risk.
- **Beta** is how much you move with the market; **alpha** is the return that beta can't explain — the skill.
- Always read **return and risk together** — a big gain on a brutal ride is luck, not quality.
</Callout>
