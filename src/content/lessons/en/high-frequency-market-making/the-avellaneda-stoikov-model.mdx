The previous lesson handed you the market maker's P&L and told you the whole job is "quote wide enough and skew cleverly enough." Lovely advice. *How* wide? Skew *how much*? In 2008 Marco Avellaneda and Sasha Stoikov wrote down the answer — the model every modern quoting engine still cribs from. It is the **F = ma** of market making: two short formulas that take your inventory, your nerves, the volatility, and the clock, and spit out exactly where to put your two prices.

By the end you'll be able to compute a bid and an ask from scratch, explain why a long maker quotes *both* prices below the mid, and recite the model's blind spots so you don't trade it like gospel.

## The setup: a utility-maximizing maker

**Analogy.** Picture a market stall owner who must keep buying and selling all day, can't go home until closing time, and *hates* being caught holding a pile of unsold stock when the bell rings. Every price tag is a bet: too generous and nobody trades with you, too greedy and you go home with a warehouse full of inventory and a heart full of regret. AS formalizes exactly that anxiety.

**The precise model.** A single maker quotes a bid and an ask over a finite horizon $[0,T]$ and wants to **maximize the expected exponential utility of terminal wealth**:

$$\max\ \mathbb{E}\!\left[-\exp(-\gamma\, X_T)\right]$$

where $X_T$ is wealth (cash plus inventory marked at the mid) at time $T$ and $\gamma>0$ is the **risk-aversion** coefficient. Exponential (CARA) utility is the trick that makes the math close: it punishes losses more than it rewards equal gains, which is precisely the fear of holding inventory.

Three modeling assumptions do the heavy lifting:

1. **The mid follows arithmetic Brownian motion** with volatility $\sigma$: $\,dS_t = \sigma\, dW_t$. No drift — the maker has no directional view, it only fears variance.
2. **Orders arrive as a Poisson process** whose intensity *decays* with distance from the mid. If your quote sits a distance $\delta$ from the mid, fills arrive at rate
   $$\lambda(\delta) = A\,e^{-k\delta}.$$
   Quote close to the mid (small $\delta$) and you get hit often but earn a thin edge; quote far (large $\delta$) and fills dry up. $k$ controls how fast that trade-off bites.
3. **Two control knobs:** the bid distance $\delta_b$ and ask distance $\delta_a$ from a center the maker gets to choose.

<Callout variant="info" title="Why exponential utility?">
A risk-*neutral* maker would just quote to maximize expected spread capture and happily warehouse unlimited inventory. The whole reason AS produces *skew* is that CARA utility makes the maker dislike the *variance* its inventory creates — so it pays to shade quotes to bleed inventory off. No risk aversion, no skew.
</Callout>

## The reservation (indifference) price

**Analogy.** The mid is the *market's* fair value. The reservation price is *your* fair value once you account for the inventory you're already carrying. If you're already long a pile of stock, you secretly value it a little *lower* than the market does — you'd love an excuse to sell — so your personal fair value sits *below* the mid. Short the stock? Your fair value sits *above* the mid because you're itching to buy it back.

**The formula.** The reservation price is

$$r(s, q, t) = s - q\,\gamma\,\sigma^2\,(T-t)$$

where $s$ is the current mid, $q$ is your net inventory (positive = long), $\gamma$ risk aversion, $\sigma^2$ variance, and $(T-t)$ time remaining. It is the price at which you'd be *indifferent* between holding your current inventory and trading one more unit — hence "indifference price."

**Worked example.** Take $s=100$, $\sigma=2$ (so $\sigma^2=4$), $\gamma=0.1$, $(T-t)=1$, and inventory $q=5$ (you're long 5):

$$r = 100 - 5\cdot 0.1\cdot 4\cdot 1 = 100 - 2 = 98.$$

Your personal fair value is **98**, a full 2 below the market's mid of 100 — because you're long and want out. Now crank the knobs:

| Change | New skew $\,q\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)$ | Reservation $r$ |
|---|---|---|
| Base ($q{=}5,\gamma{=}0.1,\sigma^2{=}4,T{-}t{=}1$) | $5\cdot0.1\cdot4\cdot1 = 2$ | 98.0 |
| Double the time ($T{-}t{=}2$) | $5\cdot0.1\cdot4\cdot2 = 4$ | 96.0 |
| Double risk aversion ($\gamma{=}0.2$) | $5\cdot0.2\cdot4\cdot1 = 4$ | 96.0 |
| Flat inventory ($q{=}0$) | $0$ | 100.0 |

More time on the clock and more risk aversion both pull $r$ *further* from the mid: the longer you must carry the position and the more you fear it, the harder you lean to shed it.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Pitfall: the reservation price is not a forecast">
$r$ is **not** a prediction that the price is heading to 98. It says nothing about where the market is going — AS assumes zero drift. The shift is purely about *your* risk: it nudges your quotes so fills tend to flatten your book, not because you think the stock is overvalued.
</Callout>

## The optimal spread

**Analogy.** The spread is your *price for taking the other side*. Charge too little and informed flow and variance eat you; charge too much and you never trade. AS shows the optimal width is the sum of two rents: an **inventory-risk rent** you charge because every fill saddles you with risk, and a **liquidity/competition rent** that depends on how quickly fills vanish as you back away from the mid.

**The formula.** The total optimal spread (ask distance plus bid distance) is

$$\delta_a + \delta_b = \gamma\,\sigma^2\,(T-t) + \frac{2}{\gamma}\ln\!\left(1 + \frac{\gamma}{k}\right).$$

- **First term, $\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)$ — the inventory/risk term.** Pure compensation for the variance you'll carry: scales with risk aversion, variance, and time left. This is the *same* quantity (per unit inventory) that skewed the reservation price.
- **Second term, $\frac{2}{\gamma}\ln(1+\gamma/k)$ — the market/liquidity term.** Comes entirely from the order-arrival model. The faster fills decay with distance (large $k$ = lots of competition, liquid name), the *smaller* this term — you can't afford to quote wide when rivals will undercut you.

**Worked example.** Use $\gamma=0.1$, $\sigma^2=4$, $T-t=1$, $k=1.5$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{inventory term} &= 0.1\cdot 4\cdot 1 = 0.4,\\[2pt]
\text{liquidity term} &= \frac{2}{0.1}\ln\!\left(1+\frac{0.1}{1.5}\right) = 20\cdot\ln(1.0667)\\[2pt]
&= 20\cdot 0.0645 = 1.291,\\[2pt]
\delta_a+\delta_b &= 0.4 + 1.291 \approx \mathbf{1.691}.
\end{aligned}
$$

So the total optimal spread is about **1.69**, and the half-spread is $\delta/2 \approx 0.845$.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Sanity check the two terms separately">
When you debug a quoting engine, split the spread into its two pieces. A spread that explodes as the day's close approaches is the *inventory* term misbehaving (it should shrink, not grow, as $T-t\to 0$). A spread that's wildly wide in a liquid name is the *liquidity* term — your fitted $k$ is too small.
</Callout>

## Placing the actual quotes

**Analogy.** You've found your personal fair value (the reservation price) and how far apart to set the two tags (the spread). Now just wrap the spread *symmetrically* around the reservation price — not around the mid. That last word is the whole game.

**The formulas.** With total spread $\delta = \delta_a+\delta_b$:

$$\text{ask} = r + \frac{\delta}{2}, \qquad \text{bid} = r - \frac{\delta}{2}.$$

**Worked example (carrying our numbers).** We had $r = 98$ and $\delta \approx 1.691$, so $\delta/2 \approx 0.845$:

$$
\text{ask} = 98 + 0.845 = 98.845, \qquad \text{bid} = 98 - 0.845 = 97.155.
$$

Look closely: the mid is **100**, yet **both** quotes — bid 97.155 *and* ask 98.845 — sit *below* the mid. The maker is long, so it has shoved its entire price ladder down to make its ask attractive (sell quickly!) and its bid unattractive (please stop selling me more!). That asymmetry around the mid *is* inventory skew, and it fell straight out of two formulas.

## Comparative statics: which knob does what

**Analogy.** Think of four dials on the quoting machine. Two outputs you care about: the **skew** $|r-s| = |q|\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)$ (how far your quotes lean off the mid) and the **spread** $\delta$ (how wide they are). Each dial pushes these two in characteristic directions.

| Turn up… | Effect on **skew** $|r-s|$ | Effect on **spread** $\delta$ |
|---|---|---|
| Risk aversion $\gamma\uparrow$ | **More skew** (lean harder to flatten) | **Wider** (inventory term up; liquidity term down — inventory usually dominates near close) |
| Volatility $\sigma\uparrow$ | **More skew** (variance is scarier) | **Wider** (inventory term grows with $\sigma^2$) |
| Time-to-close $(T-t)\uparrow$ | **More skew** (longer to carry risk) | **Wider** (inventory term grows) |
| Liquidity/competition $k\uparrow$ | **No direct effect** ($k$ isn't in $r$) | **Tighter** (the log liquidity term shrinks) |

The clean takeaway: **inventory pressure ($\gamma,\sigma,T-t$) drives both skew and width**, while **competition ($k$) only tightens the market term** and never touches your centering.

## The end-of-horizon effect

**Analogy.** A trader who must be flat by the closing bell stops fearing inventory as the bell approaches — there's no overnight gap to dread when there's no overnight. AS bakes this in through the $(T-t)$ factor.

**The mechanics.** As $t\to T$, the term $(T-t)\to 0$, so:

- the **skew** $q\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)\to 0$ — the reservation price re-centers on the mid;
- the **inventory term** of the spread $\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)\to 0$ — that half of the spread vanishes.

What's left at the very end is just the liquidity term $\frac{2}{\gamma}\ln(1+\gamma/k)$: quotes snap back symmetric around the mid and tighten to their competitive floor.

**Worked example.** Keep $q=5,\gamma=0.1,\sigma^2=4,k=1.5$ and watch $(T-t)$ run down:

| $T-t$ | Skew $=5\cdot0.1\cdot4\cdot(T{-}t)$ | Reservation $r$ | Spread $\delta$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1.0 | 2.00 | 98.00 | $0.4+1.291=1.691$ |
| 0.5 | 1.00 | 99.00 | $0.2+1.291=1.491$ |
| 0.1 | 0.20 | 99.80 | $0.04+1.291=1.331$ |
| 0.0 | 0.00 | 100.00 | $0+1.291=1.291$ |

By the bell, the maker quotes a symmetric $\pm 0.6455$ around 100 regardless of inventory. Drag the time slider to the far end of the simulator to feel the same collapse:

## From the formulas back to inventory skew

You may have noticed AS never has a rule that says "if long, shade quotes down." That behavior is *emergent*. The reservation price subtracts $q\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)$ — proportional to inventory — and the spread wraps symmetrically around it. Combine the two and the algebra *produces* the skew automatically:

$$\text{bid} = s - \underbrace{q\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)}_{\text{skew}} - \frac{\delta}{2}, \qquad \text{ask} = s - \underbrace{q\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)}_{\text{skew}} + \frac{\delta}{2}.$$

Both quotes ride the *same* skew off the mid; the spread just sets how far apart they sit. Get long, both slide down; get short, both slide up. This turns a wandering inventory into a **mean-reverting** one, because the skewed quotes make the flattening side of every trade more likely. The next lesson, *Inventory Risk & Quote Skewing*, zooms entirely into this behavior — but now you know exactly which line of algebra creates it.

## Assumptions & limitations

AS is a *model*, not a money printer. Know where it bends:

- **Constant volatility $\sigma$.** Real volatility clusters and spikes; a fixed $\sigma$ under-quotes right when you most need width (around news).
- **No explicit adverse selection.** Order arrivals are assumed *independent of the true value* — fills don't carry information. Reality disagrees: the trader lifting your ask often knows the price is about to rise. That's the Glosten–Milgrom world, a separate lesson, and AS simply doesn't price it.
- **Symmetric half-spreads in the simple form.** The closed-form approximation splits $\delta$ evenly ($\delta_a=\delta_b=\delta/2$). The exact solution lets the halves differ with inventory; practitioners often re-introduce that asymmetry.
- **Finite vs. infinite horizon.** The clean $(T-t)$ formulas assume a fixed terminal time. A continuously running maker has no natural "close," so practice uses the **Guéant–Lehalle–Fernandez-Tapia** infinite-horizon closed forms, which replace $(T-t)$ with a stationary risk term and keep quotes well-behaved indefinitely.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The biggest pitfall: AS output is not a guaranteed edge">
The model tells you the *risk-optimal* quotes given its assumptions — not that those quotes are profitable. Feed it a bad $\sigma$ or ignore adverse selection and you'll quote confidently into a buzzsaw. AS is a disciplined starting point for centering and width; the edge still has to come from good parameters, real adverse-selection control, and queue/latency execution the model never sees.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to remember">
- **Two decisions, two formulas.** Center the quotes with the **reservation price** $r = s - q\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)$; size them with the **optimal spread** $\delta = \gamma\sigma^2(T-t) + \frac{2}{\gamma}\ln(1+\gamma/k)$.
- **Quotes wrap around $r$, not the mid:** $\text{ask}=r+\delta/2$, $\text{bid}=r-\delta/2$. When long, *both* quotes sit below the mid — that's skew, and it's emergent, not a bolted-on rule.
- **Inventory pressure ($\gamma,\sigma,T-t$) drives skew and width; competition ($k$) only tightens the spread.**
- **End-of-horizon:** as $t\to T$ the $(T-t)$ terms die — quotes re-center on the mid and tighten to the liquidity floor.
- **Mind the blind spots:** constant $\sigma$, no adverse selection, symmetric halves. AS is a disciplined starting point, not a guaranteed edge — Guéant–Lehalle gives the infinite-horizon version used in practice.
</Callout>
