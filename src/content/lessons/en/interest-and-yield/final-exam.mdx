This is it — the boss fight. Three lessons taught you that the rate on the sticker is
almost never the rate in your pocket: **simple vs compound** interest, **APR vs APY**,
and **nominal vs real** returns. No new material lives here, no new tricks, no new
formulas. What lives here are *traps* — the exact misreadings that cost people money in
the real world dressed up as tempting answer choices. If you can walk through this
without face-planting on "APR always equals APY" or "a real return can't be negative,"
you've genuinely earned the badge. Sharpen up. Read every option twice.

## Theme 1 — Simple vs Compound Interest

Recall the split: **simple interest** pays only on your original **principal** (the
sum you started with), so it grows in a straight line. **Compound interest** pays on
principal *plus* the interest already added — interest on interest — so it curves
upward. And the *frequency* of compounding (annual, monthly, daily) changes the answer
even when the labeled rate is identical.

The gap is tiny over two years and brutal over thirty — that *widening* gap is the
whole personality of compounding. Now the classic frequency trap: more compounding
periods help, but the help has a hard ceiling.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The 'daily compounding doubles your money' myth">
More frequent compounding earns *more* — but only by a sliver, and it converges to a
limit, it does not explode. At a 12% nominal rate, annual compounding turns \$1,000
into \$1,120.00 in a year; daily compounding turns it into about \$1,127.47. That's an
extra **\$7.47**, not an extra thousand. Compounding frequency is a tweak lever
compared to the *rate* and the *time horizon*. Anyone promising that "daily compounding
doubles your money" is selling something.
</Callout>

### When it matters

On **short** horizons or single-period quotes, simple vs compound barely differ — which
is exactly why lenders quote whichever flatters them. On **long** horizons (a 30-year
mortgage, a retirement account), the compounding gap dominates everything. If a problem
spans many years, never reach for the straight-line "rate × years" shortcut.

## Theme 2 — APR vs APY

This is where most of the money gets lost. The **APR (Annual Percentage Rate)** is the
*nominal* rate — the headline number, compounding ignored. The **APY (Annual
Percentage Yield)**, also called the **effective annual rate (EAR)**, is the rate you
*actually* get once you fold in compounding across the year. The conversion you were
taught:

$$\text{APY} = \left(1 + \frac{\text{APR}}{n}\right)^{n} - 1$$

where $n$ is the number of compounding periods per year. APY $\ge$ APR always, and they
are *equal* only when $n = 1$ (compounded once a year).

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest

/>

<Callout variant="warning" title="'APR always equals APY' — the most expensive wrong answer">
They are equal in exactly one case: when interest compounds **once a year** ($n = 1$).
Any more often — monthly on a credit card, daily on a savings account — and the APY is
strictly *higher* than the APR. Lenders advertise the lower **APR** on what you owe;
savings products advertise the higher **APY** on what you earn. Same math, opposite
spin. If a question implies they're always identical, it's lying to you.
</Callout>

### When it matters

Comparing two products with *different compounding frequencies* — a card at 23.9% daily
vs 24% monthly, two savings accounts with different periods — is impossible using APR
alone. Convert both to **APY** and you're comparing apples to apples. The bigger $n$
gets, the more APY pulls ahead of APR, though (as in Theme 1) it converges to a ceiling.

## Theme 3 — Nominal vs Real

A number going up is not the same as your wealth going up. The **nominal rate** is the
percentage your balance grows on paper. The **real rate** strips out **inflation** (the
rate at which prices rise) to show what your money can actually *buy*. The exact link
is the **Fisher equation**:

$$1 + r_{\text{real}} = \frac{1 + r_{\text{nominal}}}{1 + r_{\text{inflation}}}$$

A handy approximation when rates are small: $r_{\text{real}} \approx r_{\text{nominal}} - r_{\text{inflation}}$.

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Guess first. Your savings earned 5% (nominal) over a year in which inflation was 3%. What is your real return (exact Fisher, two decimals)?"
  options={[
    { text: "About 1.94%", correct: true },
    { text: "Exactly 8% — you add the two rates together", correct: false },
    { text: "Exactly 2.00% — the simple subtraction is always exactly right", correct: false },
    { text: "About 15% — you multiply 5% by 3%", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Fisher: 1 + r = 1.05 / 1.03 = 1.01942, so r ≈ 1.94%. The quick subtraction (5% − 3% = 2%) is close but slightly high — it's an approximation, not the exact answer. You never add the rates (8%) or multiply them (15%)."
/>

<Callout variant="warning" title="'A real return can't be negative' — wrong, and it stings">
If inflation outruns your nominal rate, your real return is **negative** — your money
grew in number but *shrank* in buying power. Earn 2% nominal in a year with 5%
inflation and your real return is about $1.02 / 1.05 - 1 \approx -2.86\%$. You have more
dollars and can buy less with them. This is the bond-yielding-5%-leaves-you-poorer trap
from the topic intro: a positive nominal number can still be a real-terms loss.
</Callout>

### When it matters

Any time you compare returns *across* time, or judge whether you're truly getting
richer, you must go **real**. Two bonds yielding 5% are not equivalent if one lives in a
2%-inflation world and the other in a 6%-inflation world — the second is a real-terms
loss. Nominal numbers are fine for *what you'll be paid*; real numbers are the only ones
that answer *am I better off?*

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to carry out of this course">
- **The sticker rate is rarely the rate you get.** Every theme in this course is one version of that single lesson — convert the quote into what actually lands in (or leaves) your pocket.
- **Simple vs compound:** simple interest pays on principal only (straight line); compound pays on interest too (upward curve). The gap is tiny short-term and dominant long-term. Compounding *frequency* helps only marginally and converges to a ceiling — daily compounding does **not** double your money.
- **APR vs APY:** APR is the nominal rate ignoring compounding; APY $= (1 + \text{APR}/n)^n - 1$ is the true rate. APY $\ge$ APR always, equal **only** when $n = 1$. To compare products with different compounding, convert both to APY.
- **Nominal vs real:** nominal is paper growth, real is buying power. Use Fisher: $1 + r_{\text{real}} = (1 + r_{\text{nominal}}) / (1 + r_{\text{inflation}})$. A real return **can be negative** — a positive nominal yield can still leave you poorer if inflation wins.
- **The classic traps, retired for good:** APR ≠ APY (in general), real returns *can* go negative, daily compounding does *not* double anything, and the extra over simple interest is *interest-on-interest*, not inflation.
</Callout>
