## Theme 1 — What Money Is (and the Barter It Replaced)

Recall the origin story. **Barter** is trading goods directly for other goods, with no
money in between — and it only works if you find someone who wants what you have *and*
has what you want, at the same time. That's the **double coincidence of wants**, and it
"will rarely happen." **Money** is any widely accepted item used to pay for goods,
services, and debts; it turns one hard direct trade into two easy ones. Money does three
jobs: **medium of exchange** (the thing you hand over to pay), **unit of account** (the
yardstick for quoting prices), and **store of value** (it carries purchasing power into
the future). An item is "money" to the degree it does **all three at once**.

/>

The barter problem is the reason money exists at all. The next trap is subtler: lots of
things hold value, but holding value doesn't make something money.

<Callout variant="warning" title="'It holds value, so it's money' — the store-of-value trap">
A house holds value for decades, and so does gold — but neither is **money**. You can't
hand the cashier a corner of your house for coffee (a poor **medium of exchange**) and
nobody quotes prices in "fractions of a house" (not a **unit of account**). Money is the
item that does **all three** jobs at once. Being a great store of value is one blade of
the Swiss-army knife — money needs all three blades.
</Callout>

### When it matters

The three-function test is your lifelong "is this money?" detector. When a later course
asks whether crypto is money, you don't argue vibes — you score it on the three blades:
decent emerging medium of exchange, contested store of value (volatile), rarely a unit
of account (prices still quoted in fiat). Same test, every time.

## Theme 2 — What Gives Money Its Value (Fiat & Legal Tender)

Money's backing has evolved. **Commodity money** (gold or silver coins) has value from
its own material. **Representative money** is a paper claim redeemable for a commodity
held elsewhere — no intrinsic value, but a promise behind it. **Fiat money** is
government-issued currency backed by **no** commodity; its value rests on trust,
government decree, and the **network effect** (it's valuable because everyone else
accepts it). And **legal tender** is money that must be accepted to *settle a debt* —
which is narrower than people think.

/>

<Callout variant="warning" title="'Legal tender means a shop MUST take my cash' — it doesn't">
Legal-tender status governs **settling debts you already owe**, not how a seller agrees
to be paid for a **new purchase**. A shop can refuse cash for a sale and insist on a card
— legal tender doesn't force the deal. (In the US there's no federal law compelling a
private business to accept cash for a new sale.) "Legal tender" is about discharging
debts, not about commanding every cashier to take your bills.
</Callout>

### When it matters

Knowing money is backed by trust, not gold, is the key that unlocks every monetary
headline — inflation, why a currency can collapse, why a brand-new currency (including a
cryptocurrency) lives or dies by whether people *accept* it. No network, no value.

## Theme 3 — How Money Moves (Payments & Settlement)

A **payment** is a transfer of value from a **payer** (who sends it) to a **payee** (who
receives it), usually in exchange for goods or a service — value one way, goods the
other. **Cash** is a **bearer instrument**: value belongs to whoever physically holds it,
with no name or record attached — so handing over the note *is* the settlement, instant
and final. Digital payments move no physical money; **intermediaries** (your bank, a card
network) update records. The danger zone is the gap between **authorization** ("Approved"
— a promise) and **settlement** (the actual irrevocable transfer between banks), which is
**finality**.

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest

/>

<Callout variant="warning" title="'The payment went through' ≠ 'the money has settled'">
Seeing an approval is not the same as the money clearing. **Authorization** is the
promise; **clearing** is the checking-and-reconciling stage; **settlement** is when funds
actually move and the deal becomes **final** (irrevocable). Cash flips this on its head:
handing over the note *is* settlement — no clearing stage, no take-backs. With cards, the
money between banks often moves later, which is exactly where reversals and held funds
hide.
</Callout>

### When it matters

The authorization-vs-settlement gap is *the* idea behind disputed charges, pending
holds, and "the refund is processing." It's also the seed of a concept a later course
calls a blockchain **confirmation** — another world's word for "this transfer is now
final." Same intuition, new setting.

## Theme 4 — Income, Saving, Borrowing & the Ledger

Money flowing **in** is **income**; money flowing **out** is **expense**; the movement
over a period is **cash flow**. Income above expenses is a **surplus** (you can **save**
— set aside surplus, deferring consumption); expenses above income is a **deficit** (you
**borrow** — use someone else's money now, repaying later *with interest*). **Interest**
is the price paid for using money over time; the **principal** is the original amount.
And your **bank account** isn't a box of your bills — it's a **claim**: the bank owes you
that amount. The bank's record of money in, money out, and the running **balance** is a
**ledger**, and serious bookkeeping records both sides of every transfer (**double-entry**).

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Quick math. This month your income is $2,800 and your expenses are $2,500. What's the gap called, and what can you do with it?"
  options={[
    { text: "A $300 surplus — money you can save", correct: true },
    { text: "A $300 deficit — money you must borrow", correct: false },
    { text: "A $5,300 balance — you add income and expenses", correct: false },
    { text: "Nothing — income and expenses always cancel out", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Income $2,800 − expenses $2,500 = a $300 surplus. A surplus is what enables saving (deferring some consumption to later). A deficit would be the reverse (expenses above income), forcing you to borrow or draw down savings. You subtract, you don't add."
/>

<Callout variant="warning" title="'Borrowing is free money' — it's pulled-forward, with a rental fee">
Borrowing isn't extra money — it's **future income spent today**, repaid later *with
interest*. Borrow \$1,000 of **principal** at 10% simple annual interest and in one year
you repay **\$1,100**: the extra \$100 is **interest**, the price of using the money for a
year. Saving is the mirror image — lend/save \$1,000 at 10% and you *receive* \$1,100. Same
coin, two sides. Chronic deficits financed by borrowing compound against you.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="warning" title="'My exact cash sits in a vault with my name on it' — no">
A bank account is a **claim**, not a box. When you deposit \$100, the bank now **owes**
you \$100 (from its side, that's a **liability** — something it owes); it pools deposits
and lends them out. Your balance is a number the bank is obligated to honor on demand,
not a labeled stack of your specific banknotes. This is why a lost \$50 bill is gone for
good (bearer cash) but a lost debit card is usually fine — the card just *accesses* a
recorded claim.
</Callout>

### When it matters

The saving-vs-borrowing symmetry — defer consumption to *earn* interest, or pull it
forward and *pay* interest — is the hinge into the next course, where interest stops being
a named idea and becomes a computed one: the **price of time**, a dollar today versus a
dollar tomorrow. And "an account is a ledger" is the exact bridge to a blockchain: same
running-balance, double-entry intuition, just shared across many computers instead of
kept by one bank.

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to carry out of this course">
- **Money beats barter** because it kills the double coincidence of wants, and it earns the name by doing all three jobs at once: medium of exchange, unit of account, and store of value. Holding value alone (a house, gold) isn't enough.
- **Modern money is fiat** — backed by trust, government decree, and the network effect, not gold. **Legal tender** settles debts already owed; it does **not** force a shop to take your cash for a new purchase.
- **A payment** flows from payer to payee (value one way, goods the other). **Cash** is a bearer instrument — instant, final, no record. With cards, **"Approved" is authorization (a promise)**, while **settlement/finality** — the irreversible transfer between banks — usually comes later.
- **Cash flow** is income minus expenses: a **surplus** lets you **save** (defer consumption), a **deficit** forces you to **borrow** (pull consumption forward, repaid with **interest** on the **principal**). Borrowing is never free money.
- **Your bank balance is a claim**, not a box of your bills — the bank owes you that amount. The record of money in, money out, and the running balance is a **ledger**, and **double-entry** writes both sides of every transfer. A **blockchain** is the same ledger idea, shared across many computers — the bridge into the next courses.
</Callout>
