You already know a swap is a strip of forward-rate agreements — a clever way to slice it into period-by-period bets on rates. That view is great for *intuition*. But traders don't reach for a stack of FRAs when they want to price an existing swap in two seconds flat. They reach for a much slicker trick: **a swap is just two bonds wearing a trench coat.** One leg behaves exactly like a fixed-rate bond; the other behaves exactly like a floating-rate bond. Subtract them and you have the swap's value — no strip of forwards required. This lesson builds that decomposition from scratch, proves the one lemma that makes it magical (the floating leg is worth par), derives the **par swap rate** as a falling-out consequence, and then marks an off-market swap to market.

## The decomposition: a receive-fixed swap = long a fixed bond, short a floating bond

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="The fixed and floating legs only exchange coupons — neither side ever pays back the notional. So how can each leg be a 'bond,' which always repays principal at maturity?"
  options={[
    { text: 'Add a fake exchange of the notional at maturity to BOTH legs — it cancels, so the swap is unchanged, but now each leg looks like a bond', correct: true },
    { text: 'You assume the notional is repaid only on the fixed leg' },
    { text: 'Swaps actually do exchange notional, like a currency swap' },
    { text: 'You ignore the maturity date entirely' },
  ]}
  explanation="The notional is never exchanged in a vanilla rate swap. The trick is to imagine adding a notional payment to each leg at maturity. Since both legs get the same notional at the same date, they cancel exactly — the swap's value is untouched — but each leg now has a principal repayment, making it a textbook bond."
/>

**Analogy.** Picture two friends settling a recurring bill. Each month, one pays a flat \$40 (the "fixed" friend) and the other pays whatever the variable bill happens to be (the "floating" friend). Neither hands over a deposit at the start or claws one back at the end. Now imagine they *agree* that on the final month each will *also* hand the other exactly \$1,000. That extra \$1,000 flows both directions on the same day — it washes out completely. Nobody is better or worse off. But suddenly each friend's payment stream looks exactly like a loan being repaid: regular coupons, then a lump of principal at the end. That lump is the **notional**, and conjuring it on both sides is the entire trick.

**The mechanics.** A **receive-fixed** swap means you *receive* the fixed coupons and *pay* the floating coupons. Add a notional $N$ paid to you at maturity on the fixed side, and a notional $N$ paid by you at maturity on the floating side. The two cancel, so:

$$
\text{Receive-fixed swap} = \underbrace{+\,\text{fixed-rate bond}}_{\text{coupons you receive} + N} \;-\; \underbrace{\text{floating-rate bond}}_{\text{coupons you pay} + N}.
$$

A **pay-fixed** swap is the exact mirror — you pay the fixed coupons and receive floating — so it is *short* the fixed bond and *long* the floating bond:

$$
\text{Pay-fixed swap} = +\,\text{floating-rate bond} - \text{fixed-rate bond} = -(\text{receive-fixed swap}).
$$

| Swap side | Fixed bond | Floating bond | Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Receive fixed | Long (+) | Short (−) | $\text{PV}_{\text{fix}} - \text{PV}_{\text{float}}$ |
| Pay fixed | Short (−) | Long (+) | $\text{PV}_{\text{float}} - \text{PV}_{\text{fix}}$ |

<Callout variant="info" title="Why the cancellation is exactly zero, not approximately">
The added notionals are the *same number*, on the *same date*, discounted by the *same discount factor*. Their present values are therefore identical to the last decimal, and the subtraction is exact — not a rounding-tolerant approximation. That's what makes this legal: you have changed nothing about the swap's cash flows, only how you *bookkeep* them.
</Callout>

<Reveal client:visible>
<Callout variant="info" title="Quick orientation: which bond do you want to rally?">
If you receive fixed, you are **long** the fixed-rate bond. When market rates fall, a fixed-rate bond's price *rises* (its locked-in coupons now look generous). So a receive-fixed swap *gains* when rates fall — you're holding a bond that just appreciated, minus a floating bond that stays glued to par. Hold that thought; we'll quantify it in the mark-to-market section.
</Callout>

## The floating leg is worth par at every reset

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Right after its coupon resets, a floating-rate note is worth approximately…"
  options={[
    { text: 'Par (its face value / notional)', correct: true },
    { text: 'More than par, because floating coupons grow' },
    { text: 'Less than par, because of reinvestment risk' },
    { text: 'Whatever the fixed-rate bond is worth' },
  ]}
  explanation="A floating-rate note resets its coupon to the current market rate each period. Immediately after a reset, the coupon it pays equals the rate you'd discount at — so the note prices to exactly par. This is the lemma that collapses the whole swap valuation into a single subtraction."
/>

This is the keystone. **A floating-rate bond is worth par immediately after each reset** — and here's the clean reason why.

**The one-period intuition.** Suppose only one period remains. The note will pay its final floating coupon plus the notional. At the reset, that coupon is set to the current market rate $r$, so the coupon is $r \cdot N$. The note's value is everything discounted back one period at that *same* rate $r$:

$$
\text{PV} = \frac{r\,N + N}{1 + r} = \frac{N\,(1 + r)}{1 + r} = N.
$$

The $(1+r)$ on top and the $(1+r)$ on the bottom annihilate each other, leaving exactly the notional $N$ — par. The coupon was *built* from the same rate you discount with, so they can't help but cancel.

**Why it holds for many periods, not just one.** Work backwards. At the last reset the note is worth par (the one-period argument above). Step back one reset: the note pays a coupon of $r_{\text{prev}} \cdot N$ and is then worth par — so it's again $\frac{r_{\text{prev}} N + N}{1 + r_{\text{prev}}} = N$. The "worth par next reset" fact rolls all the way back to today. The floating leg is a chameleon: it always re-prices to par the instant its coupon refreshes.

**The payoff for swap pricing.** If the floating bond is worth $N$, the receive-fixed value collapses to a single term:

$$
\boxed{\;V_{\text{receive-fixed}} = \text{PV}(\text{fixed bond}) - N\;}
$$

All the work is now in pricing one ordinary fixed-rate bond and subtracting the notional. The hero island below does exactly this: it prices the fixed-rate bond at the current market rate, pins the floating bond at par, and shows their signed difference. Slide the market rate and flip the side to watch the swap drift in and out of the money.

<Callout variant="warning" title="'At par' means at a reset, between resets it drifts">
The par lemma is exact only *at the instant of a reset*. Between resets, the floating note accrues its already-fixed coupon and its value creeps slightly above par as the payment date nears, then snaps back to par at the next reset. For valuing a swap at standard reset dates this is immaterial; for an intra-period mark you carry the small accrued adjustment. The headline result — floating leg ≈ par — is the workhorse, but know it's a snapshot, not a constant.

## The par swap rate: the fixed rate that makes a new swap worth zero

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="What is special about the 'par swap rate' quoted in the market?"
  options={[
    { text: 'It is the fixed rate that makes a brand-new swap worth exactly zero at inception', correct: true },
    { text: 'It is always equal to the longest-maturity zero rate' },
    { text: 'It is the rate at which the floating leg is worth par' },
    { text: 'It is the coupon on a government bond' },
  ]}
  explanation="The par (market) swap rate is the fixed rate set so that PV(fixed bond) = par, making the swap's value zero for both sides at inception. No money changes hands to enter — that's why it's the 'fair' quoted rate."
/>

When two parties strike a *new* swap, neither should pay the other to enter — the deal must be worth **zero** at inception. From our boxed formula, $V_{\text{receive-fixed}} = \text{PV}(\text{fixed bond}) - N = 0$ requires the fixed bond to price to par. So we hunt for the coupon $c$ that makes a bond price to par.

Write the fixed bond's price using **discount factors** $DF_i$ (the present value today of \$1 paid at time $i$). With a unit notional ($N = 1$) and annual coupons $c$:

$$
\text{PV}(\text{fixed bond}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} c\,DF_i + DF_n = 1.
$$

Solve for $c$ — call it the **par swap rate** $s$:

$$
\boxed{\;s = \frac{1 - DF_n}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} DF_i}\;}
$$

With realistic **day-count fractions** $\tau_i$ (the year-fraction of each period, e.g. 0.5 for semiannual), the general form is

$$
s = \frac{1 - DF_n}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \tau_i\,DF_i}.
$$

The denominator $\sum \tau_i DF_i$ has its own name — the **annuity factor** (or PV01 building block) — and it returns over and over in swaps, so befriend it now.

**Fully worked example.** Suppose the discount factors stripped from today's curve are:

| Year $i$ | $DF_i$ |
|---|---|
| 1 | 0.9709 |
| 2 | 0.9426 |
| 3 | 0.9151 |
| 4 | 0.8885 |
| 5 | 0.8626 |

Step 1 — sum the discount factors (annual, so $\tau_i = 1$):

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{5} DF_i = 0.9709 + 0.9426 + 0.9151 + 0.8885 + 0.8626 = 4.5797.
$$

Step 2 — read off $DF_5 = 0.8626$, so $1 - DF_5 = 0.1374$.

Step 3 — the par swap rate:

$$
s = \frac{1 - DF_5}{\sum DF_i} = \frac{0.1374}{4.5797} = 0.03000 = 3.00\%.
$$

**Verify it prices to par.** Plug $c = 3.00\%$ back into the fixed bond:

$$
\text{PV} = 0.03 \times 4.5797 + 0.8626 = 0.13739 + 0.8626 = 0.99999 \approx 1.
$$

It prices to par (the tiny miss is rounding), confirming the swap is worth zero at this rate. A swap struck at 3.00% on this curve is fair to both sides.

## Marking an off-market swap to market

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest

    { text: 'Undefined until the swap matures' },

  explanation="A swap is worth zero only at inception, at the par rate. Once rates move it re-prices. The receiver pockets an above-market 4% coupon while new swaps pay only 3%, so their old swap has positive value. The pay-fixed counterparty has the mirror-image loss."
/>

The par swap rate makes a *new* swap worth zero. But the instant the curve moves, an *existing* swap stops being fair — it has gained or lost value, and somebody must report that on their books. This is **marking to market**.

**The direction of the move.** You are long the fixed bond if you receive fixed. Bonds rise when rates fall. So:

- **Receive-fixed gains when market rates FALL** (your fixed bond rallies above par).
- **Pay-fixed gains when market rates RISE** (you're effectively short that bond).

This is precisely the slider behavior in **SwapValueBridge** above: push the current market rate below the 4% contract and the receive-fixed bar turns positive (in the money); push it above and it goes negative.

**The quick approximation.** A handy shortcut values an off-market swap as the *rate differential* times the annuity times the notional:

$$
V_{\text{receive-fixed}} \approx (s_{\text{contract}} - s_{\text{market}}) \times A \times N,
$$

where $A = \sum \tau_i DF_i$ is the annuity factor and $N$ the notional. The logic: each period you receive $s_{\text{contract}}$ but the fair rate is now $s_{\text{market}}$, so you earn an excess of $(s_{\text{contract}} - s_{\text{market}})$ per period — and the annuity discounts that recurring edge back to today.

**Worked example.** A \$10,000,000 receive-fixed swap was struck at $s_{\text{contract}} = 4\%$ with 5 years left. Market swap rates drop to $s_{\text{market}} = 3\%$, and at the new curve the annuity factor is $A = 4.58$.

$$
V \approx (0.04 - 0.03) \times 4.58 \times 10{,}000{,}000 = 0.01 \times 4.58 \times 10{,}000{,}000.
$$

$$
V \approx 458{,}000 \text{ (dollars)}.
$$

The receiver is up roughly \$458,000 — they locked in a 4% stream the market will now only pay 3% for, and that 1% edge, capitalized over five discounted years, is worth nearly half a million. The pay-fixed counterparty is down the same \$458,000. (The exact bond-difference method gives a very close figure; the approximation is the trader's mental-math version.)

<Callout variant="warning" title="Misconception: 'a swap is always worth zero'">
A swap is worth zero **only at inception, and only at the par rate**. That's its defining feature *on day one* — but the very next tick of the curve breaks it. After that, an existing swap is a live, mark-to-market position with real P&L, posted collateral, and credit exposure. Treating a seasoned swap as perpetually worthless is how you miss a six-figure mark sitting on your book. "Zero at inception" is a birthday, not a life expectancy.
</Callout>

## Putting it together

Strip away the mystique and a vanilla interest-rate swap is **two bonds wearing a trench coat**. Conjure a notional payment on each leg at maturity — it cancels exactly, changing nothing — and a **receive-fixed swap becomes long a fixed-rate bond, short a floating-rate bond** (pay-fixed is the mirror). The floating leg is worth **par at every reset**, because its coupon is set to the very rate you discount with, so the value collapses to $V_{\text{receive-fixed}} = \text{PV}(\text{fixed bond}) - N$. Demanding zero value at inception forces the fixed bond to price to par, which hands you the **par swap rate** $s = (1 - DF_n)/\sum \tau_i DF_i$ for free. And the moment the curve moves the swap stops being worth zero: a receiver gains when rates fall, a payer when they rise, with a mark of roughly $(s_{\text{contract}} - s_{\text{market}}) \times A \times N$. Master this one subtraction and you can price, quote, and risk-manage a swap on a napkin.
