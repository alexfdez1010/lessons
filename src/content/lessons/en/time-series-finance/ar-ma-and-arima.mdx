The ACF and PACF told you what *kind* of memory a series has. Now we build the machines that capture it. The three letters **AR**, **MA**, and **I** — autoregressive, moving-average, and integrated — combine into the **ARIMA(p,d,q)** family, the workhorse of classical time-series forecasting. Each letter answers a different question: AR asks "how much does today depend on its own past values?", MA asks "how much does today depend on past *shocks*?", and I asks "how many times must we difference to make it stationary?" Master what each term contributes and you can read an ARIMA specification like a sentence.

## AR: today depends on its own past

**Analogy.** An autoregressive process has *inertia*. Like room temperature drifting back toward the thermostat setting, today's value is mostly an echo of recent values plus a fresh nudge. Yesterday's level predicts today's level.

**Definition.** An **AR(p)** (autoregressive of order $p$) model writes today as a weighted sum of the previous $p$ values plus a shock:
$$X_t = c + \phi_1 X_{t-1} + \phi_2 X_{t-2} + \cdots + \phi_p X_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t,$$
where $\varepsilon_t$ is white noise. The simplest case, **AR(1)**, is $X_t = c + \phi_1 X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$ — the same equation that gave us unit roots two lessons ago.

**Stationarity condition.** For AR(1), the process is stationary only if $|\phi_1| < 1$. At $\phi_1 = 1$ it's a unit-root random walk; beyond, it explodes. The coefficient $\phi_1$ *is* the persistence: $\phi_1 = 0.9$ means a shock fades slowly, $\phi_1 = 0.2$ means it's nearly forgotten by tomorrow.

**Worked example — forecasting with AR(1).** Suppose $X_t = 2 + 0.6 X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$ and today's value is $X_t = 10$.

- One-step forecast: $\hat{X}_{t+1} = 2 + 0.6 \times 10 = 8$ (set the future shock to its mean, zero).
- Two-step: $\hat{X}_{t+2} = 2 + 0.6 \times 8 = 6.8$.
- Long-run mean: solve $\mu = 2 + 0.6\mu \Rightarrow \mu = 2/(1 - 0.6) = 5$. The forecasts decay geometrically toward 5 — the unconditional mean — which is exactly the mean-reversion an AR(1) with $|\phi| < 1$ produces.

The AR signature on the diagnostic plots: **PACF cuts off at lag $p$**, ACF decays gradually.

## MA: today depends on past shocks

**Analogy.** A moving-average process has *aftershocks*. Imagine a calm pond: a stone (a shock) creates ripples that persist for a few moments and then vanish completely. Today's value is the current shock plus an echo of the *previous few shocks* — not the previous *values*. The memory is in the surprises, not the levels, and it's strictly finite.

**Definition.** An **MA(q)** (moving-average of order $q$) model writes today as the current shock plus a weighted sum of the previous $q$ shocks:
$$X_t = \mu + \varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \theta_2 \varepsilon_{t-2} + \cdots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q}.$$
The key difference from AR: an MA(q) has a **finite memory of exactly $q$ steps**. A shock today affects the next $q$ observations and then is *completely* gone — unlike AR, where a shock's influence decays forever but never fully disappears.

**Worked example — MA(1).** Suppose $X_t = \varepsilon_t + 0.8\varepsilon_{t-1}$ (zero mean). Say last period's shock was $\varepsilon_{t-1} = 3$ and this period's is $\varepsilon_t = -1$. Then $X_t = -1 + 0.8 \times 3 = 1.4$. Next period, the influence of that $\varepsilon_{t-1} = 3$ is **gone entirely** — only $\varepsilon_t$ and the new $\varepsilon_{t+1}$ matter. That's the abrupt cutoff.

The MA signature, the mirror image of AR: **ACF cuts off at lag $q$**, PACF decays gradually.

## ARMA: combining both

Many real series carry *both* kinds of memory — inertia in the levels and lingering shock effects. **ARMA(p,q)** simply stacks the two:
$$X_t = c + \sum_{i=1}^{p}\phi_i X_{t-i} + \varepsilon_t + \sum_{j=1}^{q}\theta_j \varepsilon_{t-j}.$$
It needs the series to be **stationary** already. The diagnostic signature is the giveaway that you need a blend: **both** the ACF and PACF decay gradually (neither cleanly cuts off), because the AR part muddies the MA's cutoff and vice versa. ARMA is more parsimonious than a long pure-AR or pure-MA — a low-order ARMA can capture what would otherwise need many AR or MA terms.

<Callout variant="info" title="Parsimony beats kitchen-sink fitting">
You can almost always lower the residual error by adding more AR and MA terms — but each extra parameter risks fitting noise. The discipline is to pick the *smallest* model that whitens the residuals. Information criteria (next section) formalize this: they reward fit but penalize parameter count, so a tidy ARMA(1,1) often beats a sprawling AR(8).
</Callout>

## The "I": integration and ARIMA(p,d,q)

Pure ARMA needs a stationary series — but most financial series (prices!) aren't. The **I** ("integrated") handles that by folding in the differencing from lesson 1. **ARIMA(p,d,q)** says:

1. **Difference the series $d$ times** to make it stationary (the $d$),
2. then fit an **ARMA(p,q)** to the differenced series.

So the three numbers are:

- **$p$** = autoregressive order (lags of the differenced series),
- **$d$** = number of differences needed for stationarity,
- **$q$** = moving-average order (lags of the shocks).

**Reading specifications like sentences:**

- **ARIMA(0,0,0)** = white noise (no structure).
- **ARIMA(1,0,0)** = AR(1) on an already-stationary series.
- **ARIMA(0,1,0)** = a **random walk** (difference once, model the difference as pure noise) — the canonical price model.
- **ARIMA(0,1,1)** = difference once, then MA(1) — equivalent to *exponential smoothing*, a classic forecasting method.
- **ARIMA(1,1,1)** = difference once, then a blend of one AR and one MA term on the returns.

For a stock price, you'd typically have $d = 1$ (difference the log price into log returns), then small $p$ and $q$ — because, as we know, returns are close to white noise, often leaving little for the AR/MA terms to do.

## Fitting and choosing: how many terms?

Once you've settled $d$ (difference until stationary — check with ADF and a plot), you choose $p$ and $q$. Two complementary approaches:

**1. Read the plots.** Use the cheat sheet from last lesson: PACF cutoff suggests $p$, ACF cutoff suggests $q$. Good for a first guess.

**2. Information criteria.** Fit several candidate models and compare their **AIC** (Akaike) or **BIC** (Bayesian) information criterion. Both have the form
$$\text{IC} = -2\ln(\text{likelihood}) + (\text{penalty}) \times k,$$
where $k$ is the number of parameters. The first term rewards fit; the penalty punishes complexity. **Lower is better.** AIC's penalty is $2k$; BIC's is $\ln(n)\,k$, which is harsher for large samples, so **BIC tends to pick simpler models**. Pick the specification with the lowest criterion, then **validate**: run Ljung–Box on the residuals — they should be white noise (a large p-value). If structure remains, add a term; if you're over-parameterized, drop one.

**Worked example — picking by BIC.** Three candidates on the same returns:

| Model | Parameters $k$ | $-2\ln L$ | BIC penalty $\ln(n)k$ (n=500) | BIC |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| AR(1) | 1 | 1200 | 6.2 | 1206.2 |
| ARMA(1,1) | 2 | 1188 | 12.4 | 1200.4 |
| AR(4) | 4 | 1186 | 24.9 | 1210.9 |

AR(4) fits marginally best in raw likelihood, but its parameter penalty sinks it. **ARMA(1,1)** has the lowest BIC (1200.4), so you'd choose it — the small extra fit over AR(1) justifies one extra parameter, while AR(4)'s extra terms don't earn their keep.

<Callout variant="warning" title="ARIMA models the MEAN, not the variance">
ARIMA assumes the shocks $\varepsilon_t$ are *homoskedastic* — constant variance. But financial returns have **volatility clustering**: the variance itself moves over time. ARIMA will happily fit the (near-zero) mean structure and leave the variance dynamics completely unmodeled. That's not a bug you fix inside ARIMA — it's the entire reason the next lesson exists. ARIMA for the mean, GARCH for the variance, often side by side.
</Callout>

    For prices, much of the honest answer is: you often *shouldn't* expect ARIMA to add forecasting power to the mean of liquid-equity returns — efficient markets make that ACF nearly flat, and ARIMA(0,1,0) (a random walk) is frequently the most honest model. But ARIMA still earns its place in three ways. First, **not all series are efficient-market returns**: interest rates, spreads, realized volatility, fundamentals, and macro series carry genuine, exploitable autocorrelation that ARIMA captures cleanly. Second, even for returns, the *small* surviving autocorrelations (microstructure-driven reversal at high frequency, mild momentum at lower frequency) are real and, at scale, tradable — an ARMA term can encode them. Third, ARIMA is the **mean model on top of which a GARCH variance model sits**: you fit ARIMA to whiten the mean, then model the squared residuals' clustering. So "returns are nearly white noise" narrows where ARIMA helps; it doesn't retire the tool.

## Putting it together

The **AR(p)** model writes today as a weighted sum of its own past *values* plus a shock — inertia whose persistence lives in $\phi$ (stationary only when $|\phi| < 1$); its signature is a **PACF cutoff at lag $p$**. The **MA(q)** model writes today as the current shock plus echoes of the past $q$ *shocks* — a strictly finite memory; its signature is an **ACF cutoff at lag $q$**. **ARMA(p,q)** blends both (both plots decay) and needs a stationary input. **ARIMA(p,d,q)** adds the integration step: difference $d$ times to reach stationarity, then fit ARMA — so $(0,1,0)$ is a random walk, $(0,1,1)$ is exponential smoothing, and a stock is typically $(0,1,q)$ with small $q$. You choose $d$ by testing for stationarity, then $p$ and $q$ by reading the ACF/PACF and minimizing **AIC/BIC** (lower is better; BIC favors simpler models), and you **validate** by confirming the residuals pass Ljung–Box. The one thing ARIMA does *not* touch is the variance — it models the mean and assumes constant volatility, which is exactly the gap the next lesson fills.
