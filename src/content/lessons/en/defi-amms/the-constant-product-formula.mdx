Last lesson left you with a promise: an AMM prices trades against a pool using a **formula**, and that formula is the whole magic trick. Here it is, in all its anticlimactic glory:

$$x \cdot y = k$$

Two reserves. Multiply them. Keep the answer constant. That single line decides the price of every swap, why big trades cost more, why the pool can never run dry, and how liquidity providers get paid. It's the most consequential multiplication in decentralized finance — and once it clicks, the rest of AMMs is just footnotes. Let's make it click.

## The invariant: keep the product constant

Call the two reserves $x$ and $y$ — say $x$ is the ETH in the pool and $y$ is the USDC. The pool lives by one rule:

$$x \cdot y = k$$

where $k$ is a fixed number, the **invariant**. A swap is allowed to change $x$ and to change $y$ as much as it likes — but it must leave their **product** $k$ unchanged. You put one token *in* (that reserve goes up), you take the other token *out* (that reserve goes down), and the contract picks exactly how much comes out so that the product still equals $k$.

Think of it as a seesaw with a very specific rule: the two seats can move freely, but the *area* of the rectangle they trace out is bolted to a constant. Push one side down and the other side **must** rise — and not by an equal amount, by whatever keeps the product fixed.

Plug in our numbers. A pool of **100 ETH** and **200,000 USDC** has:

$$k = 100 \times 200000 = 20{,}000{,}000$$

That 20 million is the pool's identity card. Every legal state of this pool — every combination of ETH and USDC it can be in after any sequence of swaps — sits on the curve where $x \cdot y = 20{,}000{,}000$.

<Callout variant="info" title="k is not a price">
A tempting mistake: thinking $k$ *is* the price. It isn't. $k$ just measures how much liquidity the pool holds — it barely changes. The **price** comes from the *ratio* of the reserves, which we'll do next. Two pools can share the same price but have wildly different $k$ (one deep, one shallow). Keep "product = depth, ratio = price" separate in your head.
</Callout>

## Spot price = the ratio of the reserves

So where's the price? It's the **ratio** of the two reserves. The **spot price** of ETH in terms of USDC is:

$$\text{price} = \frac{y}{x} = \frac{\text{USDC reserve}}{\text{ETH reserve}}$$

For our pool:

$$\text{price} = \frac{200000}{100} = 2000$$

One ETH is quoted at **\$2000**. Intuitively: there are 2000 USDC sitting in the pool for every 1 ETH, so the pool values them at 2000-to-1. This is the *spot* price — the price for an **infinitesimally small** trade, the slope of the curve at the pool's current point. The instant you trade a real, finite amount, the ratio shifts and the price you actually pay drifts away from it. That gap is the whole story of the next section.

## A swap walks the pool along the curve

Here's the geometric picture that makes everything intuitive. Rearrange the invariant:

$$y = \frac{k}{x}$$

That's a **hyperbola** — a curve that swoops down steeply on the left, flattens in the middle, and hugs the axes at the extremes. Every possible state of the pool is a single point sliding along this curve. A swap doesn't jump the point somewhere new; it **walks it along the hyperbola** from the old reserves to the new ones.

To swap, you sell an amount $\Delta x$ of token X into the pool. The new reserves are:

- New X reserve: $x' = x + \Delta x$ (you added X)
- New Y reserve: $y' = \dfrac{k}{x'}$ (forced by the invariant)
- Y you receive: $\Delta y = y - y'$ (the drop in the Y reserve)

Let's grind a real example. **You want to buy ETH by adding 2,000 USDC.** Here ETH is the token leaving (X) and USDC is the token entering (Y), so it's cleanest to track the USDC reserve as the one we add to:

- Old reserves: 100 ETH, 200,000 USDC, with $k = 20{,}000{,}000$.
- Add 2,000 USDC → new USDC reserve $y' = 200000 + 2000 = 202000$.
- Invariant forces the new ETH reserve: $x' = \dfrac{k}{y'} = \dfrac{20000000}{202000} \approx 99.0099$.
- ETH you receive: $100 - 99.0099 \approx 0.990$ ETH.

So 2,000 USDC bought you about **0.990 ETH**. What price did you actually pay?

$$\text{average price} = \frac{2000}{0.990} \approx 2020$$

You paid about **\$2020 per ETH** — already worse than the \$2000 spot price you saw before you traded, even though you only nudged the pool by a couple thousand dollars. And the pool's *new* spot price has moved too:

$$\text{new price} = \frac{202000}{99.0099} \approx 2040$$

The next person buying ETH starts from \$2040, not \$2000. Your trade pushed the price up, and it stays up until someone trades the other way. Drag the slider below and watch the pool point crawl along the curve — the readouts show your average price diverging from spot as the trade grows.

<Reveal client:visible buttonText="Why can't you ever drain the pool down to zero ETH?">
Because the hyperbola **never touches the axes**. Look at $y = k/x$: as you buy more and more ETH, the ETH reserve $x$ shrinks toward 0 — and $k/x$ blows up toward **infinity**. To buy ETH when only a sliver is left, you'd have to pour in a near-infinite amount of USDC, because the price ($y/x$) rockets to infinity as $x \to 0$. The same holds the other way: as $y \to 0$, the price collapses to zero. So the reserves *asymptote* to the axes but can never reach them. You can buy 99.9% of the ETH if you're rich and reckless enough, but the **last unit is mathematically unbuyable**. That asymptote is the genius of using a hyperbola: the pool is self-protecting and can never be emptied by a single trade.
</Reveal>

## Why bigger trades pay worse prices

Notice the pattern in the worked example: a tiny trade pays ≈ spot, but the price you actually get **degrades as the trade grows**. That's not a bug — it falls straight out of the curve's shape. The hyperbola is **convex**: the deeper you push a reserve, the steeper the trade-off gets, so each additional unit you buy costs more than the one before it.

A quick feel for the scale, all from the same 100 ETH / 200,000 USDC pool:

| USDC you add | ETH you get | Average price paid | New spot price |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2,000 | ≈ 0.990 | ≈ \$2,020 | ≈ \$2,040 |
| 20,000 | ≈ 9.091 | ≈ \$2,200 | ≈ \$2,420 |
| 100,000 | ≈ 33.33 | ≈ \$3,000 | ≈ \$4,500 |

Spend 1% of the pool and you barely notice (\$2020 vs \$2000). Spend half the pool and you pay 50% over spot. The lesson: **small trades ≈ spot price; big trades pay much more**, and the penalty accelerates. This worsening is the seed of **price impact / slippage**, which gets its own full lesson (lesson 4) — for now, just internalize that the curve, not a fee, is what makes size expensive.

## The fee: how LPs actually get paid

So far the math has been frictionless, and a frictionless pool would pay its liquidity providers nothing. Real AMMs add a **swap fee**. The classic Uniswap v2 fee is **0.3%**, and the mechanism is delightfully simple: the fee is skimmed off your **input** and *left inside the pool* rather than traded against the curve.

Concretely, when you add 2,000 USDC with a 0.3% fee:

- The fee is $0.003 \times 2000 = 6$ USDC.
- Only **99.7%** of your input — 1,994 USDC — actually trades against the curve and determines your ETH out.
- The 6 USDC of fee is *deposited into the pool* anyway.

Because the fee tokens stay in the pool without buying any ETH out, the pool ends each swap holding *slightly more* than the invariant strictly required — so $k$ doesn't stay perfectly constant, it **ticks up a hair every single swap**. That slow, relentless growth of $k$ is exactly the LPs' income: their share of the pool now represents a bigger pile of reserves than they put in. Every trade fattens the pool a little, and LPs own a slice of the fattening. (This is why lesson 1's "$x \cdot y = k$ stays constant" came with a quiet *almost* — fees nudge it upward.)

<Callout variant="info" title="Constant-product is one invariant, not the only one">
We've been studying the **constant-product** formula specifically — the Uniswap v2 design, where $x \cdot y = k$. It's the canonical AMM, but not the only one: pools for assets that *should* trade near each other (two dollar-stablecoins, say) use a different **stableswap** invariant that stays nearly flat through the middle to cut slippage, and other designs concentrate liquidity in price ranges. Same big idea — a formula on reserves — different curve. This lesson is the foundation they all riff on.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to remember">
- A constant-product pool obeys one rule: **$x \cdot y = k$**. A swap can change each reserve but must leave their **product** (the invariant $k$) essentially unchanged.
- The **spot price** of token X is the reserve ratio **$y/x$** — depth is the product, price is the ratio. A 100 ETH / 200,000 USDC pool has $k = 20{,}000{,}000$ and a spot price of \$2000.
- A swap **walks the pool along the hyperbola $y = k/x$**: add $\Delta x$, the invariant forces $y' = k/(x+\Delta x)$, and you receive $y - y'$. Adding 2,000 USDC buys ≈0.990 ETH at an average of ≈\$2020 — already above spot.
- The curve is **convex**, so **big trades pay much worse than spot** while tiny trades ≈ spot. That's the seed of slippage (lesson 4).
- You **can never drain the pool**: as a reserve → 0 the price → ∞, so the curve only asymptotes to the axes.
- The **0.3% fee** is skimmed from your input and left in the pool, so $k$ creeps upward every swap — which is how **liquidity providers get paid**.
</Callout>
