You can already simulate a stock by chaining random draws — that's Monte Carlo, and the engine inside it was geometric Brownian motion. But where did that engine *come from*? Why does the GBM step subtract a mysterious $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$? Why does the price turn out lognormal rather than normal? This lesson is the conceptual peak of the whole topic: we write down the **stochastic differential equation** (SDE) that defines how a price moves in continuous time, watch **ordinary calculus break** the instant it touches a Brownian path, and meet **Itô's lemma** — the one tool that repairs it, manufactures the $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ correction, and hands you GBM as a *solution* rather than a recipe you took on faith.

## What a stochastic differential equation says

A stochastic differential equation is a **recipe for the next infinitesimally small move**. Written out, the SDE for a process $X_t$ is:

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t, t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t, t)\,dW_t$$

Read it left to right as a sentence. Over the next tiny instant $dt$, the change $dX_t$ is the sum of two pieces:

- The **drift term** $\mu\,dt$ — a *predictable* pull, proportional to the length of time $dt$. This is where the process *wants* to go: the steady, deterministic lean.
- The **diffusion term** $\sigma\,dW_t$ — a *random* Brownian kick, proportional to the increment $dW_t$ of a Wiener process (the continuous-time random walk from the last lesson). This is the noise: unpredictable, mean-zero, scaling with the volatility $\sigma$.

**Analogy.** Picture a sailboat crossing a bay. There's a steady **current** carrying it in one direction — that's the drift, smooth and predictable, it acts the same every second. And there are **gusty winds** shoving it left and right at random — that's the diffusion, jittery and mean-zero. Where the boat ends up is the current's steady push *plus* the accumulated buffeting of a thousand random gusts. The SDE is just that sentence written in math: position next instant = steady push + random shove.

**Worked example — arithmetic Brownian motion.** The simplest SDE takes constant coefficients:

$$dX_t = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$$

Read it aloud: "over the next instant, $X$ drifts up by $\mu\,dt$ (a fixed lean) and gets jostled by $\sigma\,dW_t$ (a random kick scaled by volatility)." There's no $X$ on the right-hand side, so the drift and kick don't depend on the current level — the process wanders the whole real line and can go negative. That's fine for an interest-rate *spread* or a temperature, but as you'll see, it's wrong for a *price*, which is why finance reaches for a level-dependent SDE instead.

## Why ordinary calculus breaks

The chain rule you learned in school — $df = f'(x)\,dx$ — has a hidden assumption baked in: the path $x(t)$ is **smooth**. Smooth means that over a tiny step, the change is tiny in proportion ($dx$), and anything quadratic ($dx^2$) is *negligibly* tinier still, so you throw it away. That's the whole trick of differential calculus: second-order terms vanish.

Brownian motion shatters that assumption. As you saw last lesson, a Wiener path is **continuous but nowhere differentiable** — you cannot draw a tangent line to it — and it has the strange property of **quadratic variation equal to elapsed time**. In the shorthand of stochastic calculus, that's the box-calculus rule:

$$(dW)^2 = dt, \qquad (dt)^2 = 0, \qquad dt\,dW = 0$$

That first equation is the entire revolution. On a *smooth* path, $(dx)^2$ is second-order and dies. On a *Brownian* path, $(dW)^2$ is **first-order** — it equals $dt$, the same order as the drift — so it refuses to vanish. When you Taylor-expand a function $f$ of a Brownian path, the second-order term

$$\tfrac{1}{2}f''(W)\,(dW)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}f''(W)\,dt$$

does **not** disappear. It survives, contributing a real $dt$-sized drift that ordinary calculus never sees. That surviving term *is* Itô's correction. Everything else in this lesson is a consequence of those two characters: $(dW)^2 = dt$.

<Reveal client:visible>
  <details className="rounded-card border border-ink-100 bg-surface px-4 py-3">
    <summary className="cursor-pointer font-medium">Why does (dW)² = dt and not 0? (the one insight to keep)</summary>

    Chop a time interval of length $t$ into $n$ steps. Each Brownian increment has variance $t/n$, so a typical squared increment is about $t/n$. Add up all $n$ of them and you get roughly $n \times (t/n) = t$ — and crucially, as $n \to \infty$ the random scatter around that total washes out, so the **sum of squared increments converges to exactly $t$, not zero.** A smooth path's squared increments would each be of size $(t/n)^2$ and sum to $\approx t^2/n \to 0$; Brownian increments are *so much rougher* (each scales like $\sqrt{t/n}$, so its square is $t/n$) that their squares add up to a finite, non-zero total. That non-vanishing sum is **quadratic variation $= t$**, and writing it per-step gives $(dW)^2 = dt$. Hold onto this one fact and Itô's lemma stops being magic — it's just the second-order term that smooth calculus throws away but Brownian calculus can't.

## Itô's lemma and the ½σ² correction

**Itô's lemma** is the chain rule for stochastic paths. In words: when you transform a process $X_t$ through a smooth function $f$, the change $df$ is everything ordinary calculus gives you **plus one extra term** — the surviving second-order piece $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f''\,dt$. For $f(X_t)$ where $dX_t = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$:

$$df = \underbrace{\left(\mu f' + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f''\right)dt}_{\text{drift}} + \underbrace{\sigma f'\,dW_t}_{\text{diffusion}}$$

Compare it to the ordinary chain rule $df = f'\,dX = (\mu f')dt + (\sigma f')dW$. Identical except for the bonus $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f''\,dt$ glued onto the drift — the fingerprint of $(dW)^2 = dt$.

**The headline consequence for finance.** Suppose a price $S$ follows the SDE $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$ (drift $\mu$, volatility $\sigma$, both scaling with the price level). Now ask: how does $\log S$ move? Apply Itô's lemma with $f(S) = \log S$, so $f' = 1/S$ and $f'' = -1/S^2$. The drift of $\log S$ becomes $\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$. In words:

> A price with drift $\mu$ does **not** give a log-price that drifts at $\mu$. **The log drifts at $\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$.**

The curvature of the log function (it's concave — its second derivative $f''$ is negative) bends the random kicks downward on average, and Itô's term measures exactly how much: half the variance per unit time. The chart below makes it visible — slide the volatility $\sigma$ up and watch the log-price drift sink *below* the price drift, with the shaded wedge between them growing quadratically as the $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ correction balloons.

**Worked numeric example.** Take $\mu = 8\%$ and $\sigma = 40\%$. The correction is $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.40)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.16) = 0.08 = 8\%$. So the log-price drift is $\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 = 0.08 - 0.08 = 0$. Read that again: the price is *expected* to grow at 8% a year, yet its log drifts at **0%** — the *typical* (median) path is expected to go nowhere. The price's average is propped up by a few lucky exponential winners, but the median investor, riding the typical path, treads water. That entire gap is the volatility tax, and Itô's lemma is what put a number on it.

<Callout variant="info" title="Why “½σ²” and not “σ”?">
The correction is *half the **variance***, not the volatility itself. Variance ($\sigma^2$), not standard deviation ($\sigma$), is what adds up linearly over time and what the squared Brownian increment $(dW)^2 = dt$ delivers. The factor of $\tfrac{1}{2}$ is the coefficient on the second-order term of a Taylor expansion ($\tfrac{1}{2}f''$). Put together: $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$. This is why doubling volatility *quadruples* the drag, not doubles it — the wedge in the chart grows as $\sigma^2$.
</Callout>

## Geometric Brownian motion as the solution

Now everything snaps together. The SDE for a stock price is **geometric Brownian motion**:

$$dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t$$

Drift and diffusion both scale with the price $S_t$ — a percentage move, not a fixed-dollar move, which is exactly why prices stay positive and behave the same on a \$5 stock and a \$5000 stock. *Solving* this SDE (apply Itô's lemma to $\log S$, then integrate the constant-drift result) gives the closed form:

$$S_t = S_0 \exp\!\Big(\big(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\big)t + \sigma W_t\Big)$$

Look at what's inside the exponential. The log-price $\log S_t = \log S_0 + (\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)t + \sigma W_t$ is just **arithmetic Brownian motion with drift $\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$** — the same correction the chart showed. And because $\log S_t$ is normal (a constant plus a scaled Wiener value), the **price $S_t$ is lognormal**: always positive, right-skewed, with a long upside tail and a floor at zero. That's precisely the fan of paths you generated in Monte Carlo — now you know it falls straight out of the SDE.

**Worked example — mean versus median.** For a lognormal price, the **expected** price grows like $e^{\mu t}$ (the average, lifted by the lucky right-tail winners), but the **median** price grows like $e^{(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)t}$ (the typical path). With $\mu = 8\%$, $\sigma = 40\%$ over one year: the expected price multiplies by $e^{0.08} \approx 1.083$ (up 8.3%), while the median multiplies by $e^{0} = 1.000$ (flat). The mean rises while the median stays put — the unmistakable signature of a right-skewed lognormal, and the reason "expected return" and "what you'll typically get" are not the same number.

## The volatility drag, restated

Step back from the calculus and the same $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ shows up wearing a different hat: **volatility drag**, the gap between the **arithmetic** mean return and the **geometric** (compounded) growth rate. They are the same number arriving from two directions.

**The connection.** Your wealth compounds at the geometric mean, not the arithmetic mean — and the geometric mean is lower by approximately $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$. So a stock with an 8% *arithmetic* average return and 40% volatility compounds at only about $8\% - 8\% = 0\%$. The arithmetic average is the drift $\mu$; the rate your money actually grows at is $\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ — the log-drift, the median path. This is the exact same ledger you'll see again in Kelly bet-sizing and CAGR: maximizing long-run growth means maximizing $\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$, not $\mu$.

**Pitfall 1 — quoting the arithmetic mean and expecting to compound it.** People average a fund's yearly returns, get (say) 8%, and assume \$100 grows to \$108-ish a year on average over the long haul. It won't. Volatility silently skims $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ off every period's compounding. The more a return series bounces, the wider the gap between the cheerful arithmetic average they quote and the duller geometric rate their account balance actually tracks.

**Pitfall 2 — treating Itô's correction as a "fudge factor."** The $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ is not an arbitrary haircut someone bolted on to make the math work. It is the *necessary* second-order term that appears whenever you push a random process through a **curved (nonlinear) transform** — here, the log. On a *linear* transform ($f'' = 0$) the correction is exactly zero; it only bites on curvature. Far from being a fudge, it's the part of the answer ordinary calculus is structurally incapable of seeing.

## Putting it together

A **stochastic differential equation** $dX = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW$ is a recipe for the next infinitesimal move: a deterministic **drift** ($\propto dt$) plus a random **diffusion** ($\propto dW$) — a steady current plus gusty wind. Ordinary calculus breaks on a Brownian path because $(dW)^2 = dt$ refuses to vanish, so the second-order Taylor term survives. **Itô's lemma** is the chain rule that keeps that term, adding $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f''\,dt$ to the drift. The headline: a price with drift $\mu$ has a *log*-price that drifts at $\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$. Solving the GBM SDE $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$ gives $S_t = S_0\exp((\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)t + \sigma W_t)$ — log-price is arithmetic Brownian motion, so the **price is lognormal**, with the mean ($e^{\mu t}$) riding above the median ($e^{(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)t}$). And that same $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ is **volatility drag**: the tax compounding pays for bouncing around.
