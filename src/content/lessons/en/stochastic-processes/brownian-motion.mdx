You already know the random walk: a drunkard staggering left or right, one discrete step at a time. Now do something that sounds destructive but turns out to be the most important move in all of mathematical finance — **shrink the steps**. Make each step smaller, take more of them, squeeze the gaps between them toward zero. In the limit, the jagged staircase stops being a sequence of jumps and becomes a single unbroken curve that wiggles at every scale: **Brownian motion**, also called the **Wiener process**, written $W_t$. It is the continuous-time object under the Black–Scholes formula, under every diffusion model, under the geometric Brownian motion you met simulating prices. This lesson builds it from the random walk up.

## From random walk to Brownian motion

**Analogy.** Imagine filming the drunkard's walk and then speeding the film up while zooming out. Each individual lurch becomes too small to see, but the *overall* drift of his position is still there, smoothed into a continuous trembling line. Brownian motion is that limit made exact.

Here is the construction. Start with a symmetric $\pm 1$ random walk: at each tick you add $+1$ or $-1$ with equal probability. To fit $N$ steps into a time interval of length $t$, set the gap between steps to $t/N$ and **scale each step by $\sqrt{t/N}$**. Define

$$W_t^{(N)} = \sqrt{\tfrac{t}{N}}\,\sum_{k=1}^{N} \xi_k, \qquad \xi_k = \pm 1 \text{ each with probability } \tfrac12.$$

As $N \to \infty$, this jagged sum converges to **Brownian motion** $W_t$. Two facts make the limit work, and both are worth understanding precisely.

**Why $\sqrt{t/N}$ and no other scaling?** This is the whole trick. The steps $\xi_k$ are independent with variance $1$ each, so the *unscaled* sum of $N$ of them has variance $N$. Multiplying by $\sqrt{t/N}$ multiplies the variance by $(\sqrt{t/N})^2 = t/N$. So:

$$\operatorname{Var}\!\big(W_t^{(N)}\big) = \frac{t}{N}\cdot N = t.$$

The $N$'s cancel exactly. The variance lands on $t$ — finite and nonzero — for *every* $N$, and stays there in the limit. Try any other scaling and you break it: scale by $t/N$ (too small) and the variance collapses to $0$ as $N \to \infty$ (the limit is the boring constant $0$); scale by $\sqrt{t}/\,^4\!\sqrt{N}$ or anything growing faster and the variance blows up to $\infty$. Only the square-root scaling keeps a live, finite random object in the limit. That is why diffusion lives at the $\sqrt{\text{time}}$ scale and nowhere else.

**Why the limit is *Gaussian*.** Each $W_t^{(N)}$ is a scaled sum of $N$ independent identically distributed pieces. The **Central Limit Theorem** says any such sum, once standardized, converges to a normal distribution — regardless of the shape of the individual steps (here, a coin flip). So the limiting increment isn't just *some* random variable with variance $t$; it is specifically $\mathrm{Normal}(0, t)$. The CLT is the reason Brownian motion is Gaussian and not, say, uniform.

## The defining properties

Rather than re-deriving Brownian motion every time, we pin it down by four axioms. A process $W_t$ is **standard Brownian motion** if:

1. **Starts at zero:** $W_0 = 0$.
2. **Independent increments:** for non-overlapping time intervals, the changes are statistically independent. What the path does between $0$ and $1$ tells you nothing about what it does between $1$ and $2$. The future increment doesn't care about the past — there is no memory.
3. **Gaussian increments:** $W_{t+s} - W_t \sim \mathrm{Normal}(0,\, s)$. The increment over a gap of length $s$ is normal, mean zero, and **variance equal to the length of the gap**. Bigger gap, proportionally bigger variance.
4. **Continuous paths:** $t \mapsto W_t$ is a continuous function — no teleporting, no gaps.

**Worked example — the $\sqrt{t}$ law, now exact.** Property 3 with $t=0$ gives $W_t = W_t - W_0 \sim \mathrm{Normal}(0, t)$. So

$$\operatorname{Var}(W_t) = t, \qquad \operatorname{sd}(W_t) = \sqrt{t}.$$

This is the same $\sqrt{t}$ spread you saw in the random walk's $\pm\sqrt{t}$ funnel — but now it's an exact identity, not an approximation. After $1$ year the standard deviation is $1$; after $4$ years it is $\sqrt{4}=2$; to *double* the spread you need *four times* the horizon.

**Worked example — a probability.** Since $W_1 \sim \mathrm{Normal}(0,1)$, its standard deviation is exactly $1$. The chance the path lands within one standard deviation of the start at time $1$ is the familiar normal figure:

$$P(|W_1| < 1) = P(-1 < W_1 < 1) \approx 0.68.$$

And at time $t=4$, $W_4 \sim \mathrm{Normal}(0,4)$ has standard deviation $2$, so $P(|W_4| < 2) \approx 0.68$ as well — one standard deviation always captures about $68\%$, the window just widens as $\sqrt{t}$.

<Callout variant="info" title="Variance is linear in time; standard deviation is not">
The clean, additive quantity is the **variance**: over a gap of length $s$ it is exactly $s$, and variances of independent increments add. Standard deviation — the thing you actually feel as "spread" — is the square root, so it grows like $\sqrt{t}$. Confusing the two is the single most common Brownian-motion error: spread does **not** grow linearly with time.
</Callout>

## Continuous but nowhere differentiable

Here is the property that makes Brownian motion genuinely strange. Its paths are **continuous** — you can draw one without lifting your pen — yet they are **nowhere differentiable**: at no single instant does the curve have a well-defined slope. There is no $dW/dt$. Anywhere.

**Analogy — the fractal coastline.** Measure a coastline with a kilometre-long ruler and you get one length. Switch to a metre-long ruler and you find more crinkles, so the length grows. Switch to a centimetre ruler and it grows again — the coast is equally jagged at every zoom level, never smoothing into a clean tangent. A Brownian path is exactly like that in *time*: zoom into any interval and it looks just as wild and wiggly as the whole, no matter how far you magnify. It is statistically **self-similar** — a fractal curve.

Why no slope? A derivative is the limit of $\big(W_{t+h} - W_t\big)/h$ as $h \to 0$. But that increment has standard deviation $\sqrt{h}$, so the ratio has typical size $\sqrt{h}/h = 1/\sqrt{h}$, which **blows up to infinity** as $h \to 0$. The path moves too much over short intervals — like $\sqrt{h}$, not like $h$ — for any finite slope to exist.

**Why it matters.** Ordinary calculus is built on derivatives and the chain rule, and both assume your functions are smooth. Brownian motion has neither a derivative nor smoothness, so you cannot differentiate a function of $W_t$ with the rules you learned in school. This is precisely the gap that **Itô calculus** fills — a new calculus for non-differentiable, $\sqrt{t}$-scaled paths. (That's the next lesson, and the property below is its seed.)

## Quadratic variation = t

This is the single weirdest fact about Brownian motion, and the one that finance cannot live without. Chop $[0,t]$ into $n$ equal pieces and add up the **squared** increments:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n} \big(W_{t_k} - W_{t_{k-1}}\big)^2 \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{} t.$$

The sum of squared wiggles converges to $t$ — a fixed, non-random number — not to zero. People summarize this as the slogan **"$(dW)^2 = dt$"**: an infinitesimal Brownian increment, when squared, behaves like the time step itself.

**Worked intuition.** Split $[0,t]$ into $n$ pieces each of width $\Delta t = t/n$. Each increment has variance $\Delta t$, so on average its *square* is about $\Delta t$. There are $n = t/\Delta t$ of them, so the sum of squares is roughly

$$\frac{t}{\Delta t}\cdot \Delta t = t.$$

And the randomness in that sum washes out as $n$ grows (you're averaging many independent squared terms), so it doesn't just *average* $t$ — it *converges* to $t$.

**Contrast with a smooth function.** Take any differentiable $f$. Its increment over a step $\Delta t$ is about $f'(t)\,\Delta t$, so its *square* is of order $(\Delta t)^2$. Summing $n = t/\Delta t$ of those gives $(t/\Delta t)\cdot(\Delta t)^2 = t\,\Delta t \to 0$. **Smooth functions have zero quadratic variation.** Brownian motion does not — that nonzero $t$ is the mathematical fingerprint of roughness, the thing no smooth curve can produce.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Where the ½σ² in Itô's lemma comes from">
Because $(dW)^2 = dt$ instead of vanishing, a second-order Taylor term that smooth calculus would throw away survives here — it leaves behind a $\tfrac12\sigma^2$ piece. That extra term is the **Itô correction** (the same $-\tfrac12\sigma^2$ "variance drag" you met in geometric Brownian motion). Quadratic variation equal to $t$ is its seed; the next lesson makes it precise.
</Callout>

What is *not* random is how much total "squared movement" the path accumulates: that quantity, the quadratic variation, equals $t$ for **every** path with probability one. Think of it as a budget. Brownian motion is free to spend its wiggling however it likes — up, down, in any pattern — but over $[0,t]$ the total squared distance it covers is pinned at exactly $t$. The *direction* is random; the *amount of roughness* is a law of nature. That fixed budget is what gives Itô calculus its rigid, usable rules despite the underlying randomness.

## Brownian motion with drift (arithmetic BM)

Standard Brownian motion goes nowhere on average — its mean is zero forever. To model something with a *trend*, bolt a straight line onto it. **Arithmetic Brownian motion** is

$$X_t = \mu t + \sigma W_t,$$

a deterministic line $\mu t$ plus scaled Brownian noise $\sigma W_t$. Here $\mu$ is the **drift** (the average rate of climb) and $\sigma$ is the **volatility** (how violently the noise jostles). Its distribution follows immediately from the Brownian facts:

$$X_t \sim \mathrm{Normal}\big(\mu t,\ \sigma^2 t\big).$$

The mean is $\mu t$ (the line), and the variance is $\sigma^2 t$ — the Brownian variance $t$ scaled by $\sigma^2$.

**Worked example.** Let $\mu = 2$ per year, $\sigma = 3$ per year, and look at $t = 4$ years.
- **Mean:** $\mu t = 2 \times 4 = 8$.
- **Variance:** $\sigma^2 t = 9 \times 4 = 36$, so the **standard deviation** is $\sqrt{36} = 6$.
- So $X_4 \sim \mathrm{Normal}(8, 36)$. A one-standard-deviation band runs from $8 - 6 = 2$ up to $8 + 6 = 14$, and roughly $68\%$ of outcomes fall inside it.
- Notice $P(X_4 < 0)$ is **not** zero: that's about $1.33$ standard deviations below the mean, a chance of roughly $9\%$. The process can — and sometimes does — go negative.

That last point is the whole pitfall.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Why prices don't use arithmetic Brownian motion">
Arithmetic BM has two flaws as a price model. **(1) It can go negative** — its noise is unbounded normal, so enough bad draws push $X_t$ below zero, and a stock can't cost minus four dollars. **(2) Its shocks are fixed-size in dollars** — a volatility σ of \$3 means a roughly \$3 daily wiggle whether the asset trades at \$10 or \$10,000, which is absurd: real assets move in *percentages*, not fixed dollars. Finance fixes both by switching to **geometric** Brownian motion — the exponential of an arithmetic BM, $S_t = S_0\,e^{X_t}$ — so prices stay strictly positive and move in proportional (percentage) terms. That's the GBM you simulated in the Monte Carlo course, and the next lesson derives it with Itô.
</Callout>

## Putting it together

Brownian motion is the **scaling limit of a random walk**: shrink the $\pm 1$ steps by $\sqrt{t/N}$, take $N \to \infty$, and the Central Limit Theorem hands you a continuous Gaussian process $W_t$. It is defined by four axioms — start at zero, independent increments, $\mathrm{Normal}(0,s)$ increments over a gap $s$, continuous paths — from which $\operatorname{Var}(W_t)=t$ and the $\sqrt{t}$ spread fall out. Its paths are continuous but **nowhere differentiable** (too rough for any slope, which is why Itô calculus exists), and their **quadratic variation equals $t$** — the "$(dW)^2 = dt$" fact that seeds the $\tfrac12\sigma^2$ Itô term. Add a trend and you get **arithmetic Brownian motion** $X_t = \mu t + \sigma W_t \sim \mathrm{Normal}(\mu t, \sigma^2 t)$ — useful, but it can go negative and adds fixed-dollar noise, which is why prices use *geometric* BM instead.
