## Theme 1 — What an Asset Is

Recall the split: an **asset** is something you *own* that puts money in your pocket or
can be sold for more later — it either pays you **income** (rent, dividends, interest)
or **appreciates** (rises in value). A **liability** does the opposite: it pulls money
*out* of your pocket. Ownership matters too — a **claim** (lending money, holding a
bond) is not the same as **owning** the thing. And **liquidity** is how fast you can
turn something into cash without slashing the price.

The myth that trips everyone is "anything expensive is an asset." Price tag is
irrelevant — *cash-flow direction* is everything. Now separate the two ways a real asset
can actually make you money.

<Callout variant="warning" title="'Anything expensive is an asset' — the most common beginner mistake">
An asset is defined by what it *does to your cash flow*, not by its sticker price. A
\$40,000 financed car that depreciates and demands payments is a **liability**. A
\$2,000 dividend-paying share is an **asset**. The dividing line is simple: does it put
money *in* your pocket (income or appreciation) or pull money *out*? Expensive liabilities
are still liabilities — that's exactly how they sneak onto the wrong side of the ledger.
</Callout>

### When it matters

Every financial decision starts with reading the ledger correctly. If you mislabel a
liability as an asset, you'll feel "wealthy" while cash quietly leaks out. Before you
admire something's price, ask the only question that counts: which direction does the
money flow?

## Theme 2 — Saving vs Investing

**Saving** is parking money somewhere safe and liquid for short-term needs and
emergencies; the dollar amount won't fall, but it earns little. **Investing** is putting
money to work in assets that can grow over the long run, accepting that the value bounces
around in the short term. The order is non-negotiable: build an **emergency fund first**,
*then* invest. And the choice is driven by **time horizon** — match the tool to when
you'll need the money. The hidden enemy of idle cash is **inflation**, which quietly
erodes its buying power.

money belongs in savings, not the market. Over a **long** horizon, the bouncing tends to
smooth out and growth wins. The flip side trap: a savings account feels "safe," but
parked cash steadily loses **buying power** to inflation. Safe-in-dollars is not the same
as safe-in-value.
</Callout>

### When it matters

Get the order wrong and you'll be forced to sell investments at the worst possible
moment to cover an emergency — locking in losses. Match the horizon to the tool: near-term
money stays safe and liquid; long-term money goes to work. And never mistake "the dollar
number can't fall" for "I'm not losing anything" — inflation is always charging rent on
idle cash.

## Theme 3 — Risk & Return

The iron law: **no free lunch**. Higher expected returns come *only* by accepting more
**risk** (more bouncing around, a real chance of loss). A crucial subtlety — the *same*
average return can hide *very different* risk, so two investments that both "made 8%" are
not equivalent. Assets sit on a **risk ladder** (cash → bonds → broad stock funds →
single stocks → speculative bets), and a longer **time horizon** tames risk by giving the
bounces time to average out.

<Callout variant="warning" title="'More volatile always means more money' — no, it means more uncertainty">
Higher risk raises your *expected* return, not your *guaranteed* one — and 'expected'
includes the very real chance of loss. A wildly volatile asset can absolutely end up
*below* a calm one; volatility is the price of admission, never a receipt for profit.
Two assets can post the same average return while one rode a gentle slope and the other a
roller coaster — same destination, very different odds of getting wiped out along the way.
Judge return *and* risk together, never return alone.
</Callout>

### When it matters

Any time someone quotes you a return, your first question is *how much risk came with
it?* A 12% return from a diversified fund and a 12% return from one lucky stock are not
the same thing — the second carries a far larger chance of a deep loss. And the longer
your horizon, the more risk you can sensibly take, because time is volatility's antidote.

## Theme 4 — Stocks, Bonds & Funds

Three building blocks, three different relationships. A **stock (share)** makes you a
part-**owner** of a company — you ride its upside and its downside. A **bond** makes you
a **lender**: you're owed fixed payments and your principal back, but you don't own the
company. A **fund** (like an index fund) is a single basket holding many stocks and/or
bonds at once, handing you instant **diversification**. And when a company runs into
trouble, the **payment order** matters: bondholders (lenders) get paid before
stockholders (owners).

/>

<Callout variant="warning" title="'A single hot stock is safer than an index fund' — backwards">
A single stock concentrates everything on *one* company — one bad quarter, one scandal,
and a big chunk of your money is gone. An index fund spreads your money across *hundreds*
of companies, so any single failure barely dents you. Diversification is the closest
thing to a free win in investing, and a fund hands it to you in one purchase. A 'hot'
stock might soar — but 'might soar' is the opposite of 'safer.' Concentration is risk;
spreading out is safety.
</Callout>

### When it matters

Knowing what each instrument makes you tells you who gets paid, who carries the risk, and
how concentrated you are. Reach for a single stock and you're betting on one company;
reach for a fund and you're buying the whole crowd. For a beginner building wealth over
decades, that difference — concentration vs diversification — is one of the highest-stakes
choices on the board.

## Theme 5 — What a Return Means

A **return** is how much your investment grew (or shrank), expressed as a **percentage**
of what you put in. It has two parts that you must add together: the **capital gain**
(the price change) *plus* the **income** (dividends or interest paid out). So **total
return = capital gain + income**. Two non-negotiable rules: returns can be **negative**
(you can lose money), and you compare investments by **percentage, not raw dollars** —
because a \$100 gain means something completely different on \$200 than on \$200,000.

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="Do the arithmetic. You buy a stock for $100. A year later it's worth $108 and it paid you $4 in dividends along the way. What's your total return?"
  options={[
    { text: "12% — the $8 price gain plus the $4 dividend is $12 on $100", correct: true },
    { text: "8% — only the price change counts as return", correct: false },
    { text: "4% — only the dividend counts; price changes don't", correct: false },
    { text: "$12 — return is the dollar amount you made", correct: false },
  ]}
  explanation="Total return = capital gain + income = ($108 − $100) + $4 = $8 + $4 = $12, and $12 / $100 = 12%. You must include BOTH the price change and the dividend, and you express it as a percentage — not just $12, which means nothing without knowing what you invested."
/>

<Callout variant="warning" title="'The bigger dollar gain is the better return' — not without the percentage">
A \$1,000 gain sounds better than a \$100 gain — until you learn the first came from a
\$100,000 investment (a 1% return) and the second from a \$500 investment (a 20% return).
Raw dollars hide how hard your money worked; **percentages** reveal it. Always compare
returns as a *percentage* of what you invested, and never forget that returns can go
**negative** — a price that falls turns your return into a real loss.
</Callout>

### When it matters

Every headline number you'll ever see — a fund's "return," a stock's "performance" — is
this calculation. Miss the income piece and you'll undercount what you actually earned;
miss the percentage piece and you'll be fooled by big dollar figures on big balances. And
the moment you forget returns can go negative, you've stopped doing real investing math.

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to carry out of this course">
- **Read the ledger, not the price tag.** An asset puts money *in* your pocket (income or appreciation); a liability pulls it *out*. An expensive, financed, depreciating car is a liability — cost has nothing to do with it.
- **Save before you invest.** Build a liquid emergency fund first, then invest money you won't need for years. Match the tool to your time horizon, and remember idle cash quietly loses buying power to inflation.
- **No free lunch.** Higher expected returns require accepting more risk, and 'expected' includes a real chance of loss. The same average return can hide very different risk — judge return *and* risk together. A longer horizon tames volatility.
- **Know what each instrument makes you.** A stock makes you an *owner*; a bond makes you a *lender* who's paid before owners; a fund is a *basket* that hands you instant diversification. A single hot stock is *more* concentrated, not safer, than a broad index fund.
- **Read returns correctly.** Total return = capital gain + income (don't forget the dividends or interest). Returns *can* go negative, and you compare investments by *percentage* of what you invested — never by the raw dollar gain.
</Callout>
