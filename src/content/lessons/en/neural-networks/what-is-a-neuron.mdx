An artificial **neuron** is the smallest decision-maker in a neural network. It
takes several inputs, decides how much each one matters, adds them up, and
produces a single output. That's it — everything else is repetition and scale.

## The weighted sum

Each input `x` is multiplied by a **weight** `w` that says how important it is.
We add a **bias** `b` to shift the result, then sum everything:

> z = (x₁·w₁ + x₂·w₂ + … + xₙ·wₙ) + b

The weights are what the network *learns*. Training nudges them up or down until
the neuron's output matches what we want.

<Callout variant="info" title="Weights are the knobs">
  A neuron starts with random weights and is essentially useless. Learning is
  just the slow, automatic tuning of these knobs until the answers come out
  right.
</Callout>

## Firing: the threshold

After computing `z`, the neuron passes it through an **activation function** that
decides how strongly to "fire". The simplest one is a step: fire (1) if `z`
crosses a threshold, stay quiet (0) otherwise.

  <Callout variant="tip" title="Why a bias?">
    Without a bias, every neuron is forced through the origin — it can only
    represent relationships that pass through zero. The bias lets the neuron
    shift its decision boundary anywhere it needs.
  </Callout>
</Reveal>
