Last lesson, duration handed you a clean one-liner: a bond's price moves about minus-duration times the change in yield. Tidy. Linear. And вҖ” for anything bigger than a baby rate move вҖ” a little bit *wrong*. The true priceвҖ“yield relationship isn't a straight line at all; it's a gently bowed **curve**, and duration is just the straight ruler we laid tangent to it at today's yield. The further yields wander from today, the more the real curve peels away from that ruler. Here's the lovely part: it always peels away in the *holder's favour*. That bend has a name вҖ” **convexity** вҖ” and once you can see it, you'll never trust a bare duration estimate on a big rate move again.

## Duration is a tangent line, not the truth

Picture a smooth hill in profile. Stand at one spot and lay a straight plank flat against the slope so it just *kisses* the ground at your feet вҖ” that plank is a **tangent line**. Right where you're standing, the plank and the hill agree almost perfectly. Take a few steps in either direction, though, and the hill curves away while the plank keeps going dead straight; the gap between them grows the further you walk.

That's exactly the relationship between a bond's price and its yield. The **priceвҖ“yield curve** вҖ” actual price plotted against yield вҖ” is a downward-sloping *curve* that bends (it's **convex**, meaning it bows so it always sits *above* its own tangent line). **Duration** is the tangent: the straight-line approximation that's spot-on at today's yield and drifts off as yields move. Formally, duration's estimate of the fractional price change is

$$\%\Delta P \approx -D \cdot \Delta y$$

where $D$ is **modified duration** (roughly, the bond's percentage price sensitivity per 1-point yield change) and $\Delta y$ is the change in yield as a decimal. It's the equation of a straight line вҖ” so by construction it can't capture a curve.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Duration is only exact at one point">
The single most common slip is treating the duration line as the bond's *true* price curve everywhere. It isn't вҖ” it's only exact at today's yield, where the line touches the curve. For tiny yield wiggles the line and the curve are practically the same, so duration alone is fine. For a big move the curve has visibly peeled away, and a duration-only number is measurably off.
</Callout>

### When it matters

For pocket-change yield moves (a few basis points), the tangent hugs the curve and you can stop at duration. The mismatch only becomes worth fixing once yields move *a lot* вҖ” exactly the scenarios (rate shocks, long bonds) where you most need the number to be right.

## The curve always bows the holder's way

Here's the asymmetry that makes convexity worth caring about. Because the price curve sits *above* its tangent on both sides, the actual price is **always above the duration estimate** вҖ” no matter which way yields move.

Read off what that means in each direction:

- **Yields rise** вҶ’ both line and curve predict a *loss*, but the curve is higher, so the **real loss is smaller** than duration claimed. Duration **overestimates** your loss.
- **Yields fall** вҶ’ both predict a *gain*, but the curve is again higher, so the **real gain is bigger** than duration claimed. Duration **underestimates** your gain.

Lose a little less when rates go against you; gain a little more when they go your way. That's an unambiguously good deal for the bondholder, and it's why **positive convexity is a desirable property**: the bend bails you out on the downside and pads you on the upside. ### When it matters

The asymmetry is the whole reason convexity gets *priced*: investors will pay a hair more (accept a slightly lower yield) for a bond with more convexity, because that favourable bend is genuinely valuable. When two bonds look identical on duration, convexity is the tiebreaker вҖ” more on that below.

## The convexity correction term

So how do we put a number on the bend? We add one more term to the straight-line estimate. The improved formula is a **second-order approximation** вҖ” duration captures the straight slope; convexity captures the curvature:

$$\%\Delta P \approx -D \cdot \Delta y + \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot C \cdot (\Delta y)^2$$

Here $C$ is the bond's **convexity**, a measure of *how much* the priceвҖ“yield line bends. Two things to notice. First, the correction uses $(\Delta y)^2$ вҖ” yield change *squared* вҖ” so it's tiny for small moves and grows fast for big ones; that's exactly why convexity only matters when yields move a lot. Second, because $(\Delta y)^2$ is always positive (a negative times a negative is positive) and convexity is positive, the correction term is **always a plus**. It nudges the estimate *up* whether yields rose or fell вҖ” pushing the straight-line guess toward the higher true curve in both directions. That single always-positive nudge is the math behind "lose less, gain more."

### Worked example вҖ” yields rise 2%

Take a bond with **modified duration $D = 7$** and **convexity $C = 70$**, and shock yields **up** by $\Delta y = +0.02$ (a 2-percentage-point jump). Step through both pieces:

**Duration-only piece:**

$$-D \cdot \Delta y = -7 \cdot 0.02 = -0.14 = -14.0\%$$

The straight line says the bond loses 14%. **Convexity correction:**

$$\tfrac{1}{2} \cdot C \cdot (\Delta y)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 70 \cdot (0.02)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 70 \cdot 0.0004 = +0.014 = +1.4\%$$

Add them:

$$\%\Delta P \approx -14.0\% + 1.4\% = -12.6\%$$

So the *real* loss is about **12.6%**, not the scary 14% duration alone shouted. Convexity bought back **1.4 percentage points** вҖ” duration overestimated the loss by exactly that much.

### Worked example вҖ” yields fall 2% (the mirror)

Now the flip side: same bond, yields **down** by $\Delta y = -0.02$.

**Duration-only piece:**

$$-D \cdot \Delta y = -7 \cdot (-0.02) = +0.14 = +14.0\%$$

**Convexity correction** вҖ” note $(\Delta y)^2 = (-0.02)^2 = 0.0004$, *still positive*:

$$\tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 70 \cdot (-0.02)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 70 \cdot 0.0004 = +0.014 = +1.4\%$$

Add them:

$$\%\Delta P \approx +14.0\% + 1.4\% = +15.4\%$$

The real gain is about **15.4%**, *bigger* than the 14% the straight line promised. Same 1.4-point convexity bonus вҖ” but this time it's added *on top of* a gain. Lose 12.6% instead of 14% when rates rise; gain 15.4% instead of 14% when they fall. Asymmetry, in your favour, both ways.

## Reading it off a table

Lining up duration-only, duration-plus-convexity, and the actual price across several yield moves shows the pattern at a glance. Same bond ($D = 7$, $C = 70$):

| Yield move $\Delta y$ | Duration only ($-D\,\Delta y$) | Convexity term ($\tfrac12 C \,\Delta y^2$) | Duration + convexity | Actual (stated) | Duration-only error |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $-0.02$ (down 2%) | $+14.0\%$ | $+1.4\%$ | $+15.4\%$ | $\approx +15.4\%$ | understates gain by 1.4 |
| $-0.01$ (down 1%) | $+7.0\%$ | $+0.35\%$ | $+7.35\%$ | $\approx +7.35\%$ | understates gain by 0.35 |
| $0$ | $0.0\%$ | $0.0\%$ | $0.0\%$ | $0.0\%$ | exact |
| $+0.01$ (up 1%) | $-7.0\%$ | $+0.35\%$ | $-6.65\%$ | $\approx -6.65\%$ | overstates loss by 0.35 |
| $+0.02$ (up 2%) | $-14.0\%$ | $+1.4\%$ | $-12.6\%$ | $\approx -12.6\%$ | overstates loss by 1.4 |

Three things jump out. The duration-only column is **symmetric** вҖ” flip the sign of $\Delta y$ and the magnitude is identical ($\pm 7\%$, $\pm 14\%$). But the actual prices are **asymmetric**: the upside ($+15.4\%$) is bigger than the downside ($-12.6\%$) for the same-size move. And the duration-only error **grows with the square of the move** вҖ” quadruple at $\pm 0.02$ what it is at $\pm 0.01$ (1.4 vs 0.35), because the correction rides on $(\Delta y)^2$. Tiny moves: barely any error. Big moves: the gap is real money.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Skipping convexity on a big move hides the asymmetry">
For small yield wiggles, ignoring convexity costs you almost nothing вҖ” the correction is a rounding error. The danger is using *duration alone* for a **large** rate move: you'll get a symmetric, too-pessimistic-on-the-downside, too-stingy-on-the-upside picture and completely miss the favourable asymmetry. The bigger the move, the more the missing $\tfrac12 C (\Delta y)^2$ term matters вҖ” and it's exactly the big moves where being wrong is expensive.
</Callout>

### When it matters

Convexity earns its keep in three places: **big rate moves** (the squared term finally bites), **long-maturity bonds** (which carry both high duration *and* high convexity, magnifying everything), and **comparing two bonds with equal duration** вҖ” where convexity is the only thing left to tell them apart.

## Two bonds, same duration

Suppose two bonds have *identical* modified duration of 7 вҖ” so duration alone calls them interchangeable. Bond A has convexity 40; Bond B has convexity 90. Shock yields $\pm 0.02$ and watch them diverge:

| | Bond A ($C = 40$) | Bond B ($C = 90$) |
|---|---|---|
| Convexity term at $\Delta y = 0.02$ | $\tfrac12 \cdot 40 \cdot 0.0004 = +0.8\%$ | $\tfrac12 \cdot 90 \cdot 0.0004 = +1.8\%$ |
| Yields **up** 2% (loss) | $-14\% + 0.8\% = -13.2\%$ | $-14\% + 1.8\% = -12.2\%$ |
| Yields **down** 2% (gain) | $+14\% + 0.8\% = +14.8\%$ | $+14\% + 1.8\% = +15.8\%$ |

Bond B loses **less** when rates rise *and* gains **more** when they fall вҖ” it dominates Bond A in both directions, purely on its higher convexity. That's why, all else equal, **more convexity is more desirable**, and why the market quietly *prices it in*: a more-convex bond typically trades at a slightly richer price (slightly lower yield). You don't get that favourable bend for free вҖ” but when you're choosing between two equal-duration bonds, it's the deciding feature.

<Callout variant="info" title="A quick reality check on the price of convexity">
Because convexity is valuable, you rarely find a high-convexity bond going cheap by accident вҖ” its appeal is already baked into its price. Convexity isn't a free lunch; it's a fairly-priced feature. The lesson is to *recognise* it so you're comparing bonds on equal footing, not to expect to pocket it for nothing.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to remember">
- **The true priceвҖ“yield relationship is a curve, not a line.** Duration is only the straight **tangent** to that curve at today's yield вҖ” exact right there, off everywhere else.
- **The curve bows above its tangent**, so the actual price is **always above** the duration estimate. Duration therefore **overestimates losses** when yields rise and **underestimates gains** when yields fall.
- **That favourable bend is convexity, and positive convexity is good for the holder** вҖ” you lose a little less and gain a little more than the straight line says.
- **Better estimate:** $\%\Delta P \approx -D \cdot \Delta y + \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot C \cdot (\Delta y)^2$. The correction rides on $(\Delta y)^2$, so it's **always positive** and grows with the *square* of the move.
- **Worked anchor:** $D = 7$, $C = 70$. Yields up 2%: $-14\% + 1.4\% = -12.6\%$. Yields down 2%: $+14\% + 1.4\% = +15.4\%$. Lose less, gain more.
- **When it matters:** big rate moves, long bonds, and choosing between two **equal-duration** bonds вҖ” where more convexity wins. It's valuable, so the market **prices it in**; it's a feature, not a free lunch.
</Callout>
