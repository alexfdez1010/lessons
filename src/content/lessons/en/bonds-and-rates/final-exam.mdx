## Theme 1 — What a bond is

Recall the core: a **bond** is a **loan** you make to a government or company, not a slice of ownership. You hand over the **face value** (also called *par* — usually \$1,000, the amount repaid at the end), you collect fixed **coupon** payments along the way, and you get the face value back at **maturity** (the end date). The coupon is a *percentage of face value, fixed at issue* — it never moves when the bond's market price moves. A stock makes you an **owner**; a bond makes you a **lender**.

The coupon cash is nailed to the face value forever; only the *price you pay* and therefore your *yield* move in the market. Now the ownership trap — bonds and stocks are not two flavors of the same thing.

<Callout variant="warning" title="A bond is a loan, not a share">
The single most common beginner error: treating a bond like a stock. A **shareholder owns**
a piece of the company and shares in its upside and votes; a **bondholder lent** money and is
owed fixed payments plus the face value back — full stop, no upside beyond that, no vote.
Because they get paid *before* shareholders if the company fails, bonds are generally lower
risk and lower reward. "The coupon is a slice of profits" and "a higher price means a bigger
coupon" are both the same mistake: confusing a lender's fixed claim with an owner's variable one.
</Callout>

### When it matters

It matters the instant you compare a bond to a stock, or read a quote. If you think the coupon floats with price, you'll mis-state your income; if you think a bond is ownership, you'll mis-judge its risk and where you stand if the issuer defaults. Lender-versus-owner is the foundation every later theme is built on.

## Theme 2 — The price-yield seesaw

A bond's coupon cash is *fixed*, but the **yield** — the return a buyer actually earns — depends on the *price they pay*. Pay less for the same fixed payments and your yield goes up; pay more and it goes down. So **price and yield move in opposite directions**: a seesaw. The cleanest mental model is the **current yield**:

$$\text{Current yield} = \frac{\text{annual coupon cash}}{\text{price}}$$

When market rates rise, *new* bonds pay more, so the price of *old* lower-coupon bonds must fall until their yield matches — and vice versa.

/>

Buy the same \$50-a-year stream for less and you've earned a higher return — obvious once you see it, lethal if you forget the direction.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Yields up does NOT mean prices up">
The reflex that wrecks beginners: "rates went up, so my bonds went up." Backwards. When yields
rise, the prices of bonds you already hold **fall** — that's the whole seesaw. And "coupon
equals yield" is only true at par (price = face value). A bond bought above face value (a
*premium*) yields *less* than its coupon rate; bought below face value (a *discount*) it yields
*more*. The fixed coupon is the constant; price and yield are the two ends of the seesaw, always
tipping opposite ways.
</Callout>

### When it matters

It matters every time rates move in the news. Holding a bond and rates jump? Your bond's *market* price just dropped — even though it still pays the same coupon and still repays par at maturity. The seesaw is also why "safe" bonds can post losses in a rising-rate year: the cash flows are safe, but the *price* in the meantime is not.

## Theme 3 — The yield curve

Line up the yields of bonds from the *same issuer* (usually government bonds) by their **maturity** — 3 months, 2 years, 10 years, 30 years — and connect the dots. That line is the **yield curve**: a *snapshot* of many different bonds at one moment, not one bond tracked over time. Normally it slopes **upward** (longer loans pay more, to compensate for tying up money longer and bearing more risk). When short-term yields rise *above* long-term yields, the curve is **inverted** — and inversion has historically *preceded* many recessions, though it does not cause or guarantee one.

Normal, flat, inverted — the *shape* is the signal markets read. But a leading indicator is not a crystal ball.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The curve is a snapshot, and inversion is a warning, not a verdict">
Two traps in one. First, the yield curve is **not one bond aging over time** — it's a lineup of
*different* bonds (different maturities) priced at the *same* moment. Second, an **inverted**
curve (short yields above long yields) has *preceded* many recessions, which makes it a watched
*leading indicator* — but it does **not guarantee** one, and its timing is loose (sometimes a
year or more ahead, sometimes a false alarm). Treat inversion as a flashing yellow light, never a
crystal ball.
</Callout>

### When it matters

It matters when you're judging the *economy's mood* from the bond market, or deciding how long to lend. An upward curve rewards going long; a flat or inverted curve says the market expects rates (and growth) to fall ahead. Read the *shape*, not a single point — and never confuse a snapshot of many bonds with one bond's journey through time.

## Theme 4 — Duration

A bond's price moves when yields move — but *how much*? That's **duration**: the bond's price sensitivity to a change in yield, quoted as an **approximate percent price change per 1 percentage-point (1%) change in yield**. The key form is **modified duration**: a modified duration of $D$ means

$$\%\,\Delta\text{Price} \approx -D \times \Delta y$$

where $\Delta y$ is the yield change in percentage points. The minus sign is the seesaw from Theme 2. Longer maturities and *lower* coupons mean *longer* duration (more sensitivity); higher coupons return your money sooner, shortening duration.

<MCQ
  client:visible
  pretest
  question="A bond has a modified duration of 7. Yields rise by 1 percentage point (1%). What is the approximate price change?"
  options={[

    { text: "A drop of about 1%, because yields only moved 1 point", correct: false },
    { text: "A rise of about 7%, because higher yields lift bond prices", correct: false },
    { text: "A drop of about 7 dollars, since duration is measured in dollars", correct: false },

  explanation="%ΔPrice ≈ −D × Δy = −7 × 1% = −7%. Duration is a PERCENT sensitivity (per 1% yield change), not a dollar amount and not the yield change itself. The minus sign is the price-yield seesaw: yields up → price down, here by about 7%."
/>

Duration converts a yield wiggle into a price move with one multiplication — that's its whole job.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Duration is a percent sensitivity, and a lower coupon makes it LONGER">
Two traps. First, duration is **not measured in percent of nothing or in dollars** — it's a
*multiplier*: percent price change *per 1 percentage point* of yield change. A duration of 7 says
"~7% price move per 1% yield move," not "7%" and not "\$7." Second, **higher coupon ≠ longer
duration** — it's the reverse. A higher coupon hands your cash back sooner, so the bond is *less*
sensitive (shorter duration); a *low* coupon (or a zero-coupon bond) leaves your money tied up
longest, giving the *longest* duration. More time-to-money means more rate risk.
</Callout>

### When it matters

It matters whenever you're sizing **interest-rate risk**. Two bonds can yield the same and feel completely different in a rate shock: a duration-2 bond barely flinches when yields jump 1% (~2% price move), while a duration-15 long bond lurches ~15%. Pick short duration when you fear rising rates; reach for long duration when you expect them to fall (and want the price kick).

## Theme 5 — Convexity

Duration draws a *straight line*, but the true price-yield relationship is a *curve* that bows toward the holder. **Convexity** measures that curvature — the bit duration's straight-line estimate leaves out. Its gift: prices **rise more** than duration predicts when yields fall, and **fall less** than duration predicts when yields rise. The fuller estimate adds a convexity term:

$$\%\,\Delta\text{Price} \approx -D \times \Delta y + \tfrac{1}{2} \times C \times (\Delta y)^2$$

That $(\Delta y)^2$ term is always *positive* (a square), so convexity always works *in the holder's favor* — and it matters most for *big* yield moves, where the straight line drifts furthest from the curve.

/>

For small moves duration alone is fine; for big moves you *need* the convexity boost, and it's always a bonus, never a penalty.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Convexity HELPS the holder, and duration alone fails on big moves">
Two traps to retire. First, **convexity is good for the holder**, not bad. Because its term
$\tfrac{1}{2} C (\Delta y)^2$ squares the yield change, it's *always positive* — your gains run
*bigger* and your losses run *smaller* than the straight-line duration estimate predicts. Second,
**duration is not exact for large yield moves** — it's a *first-order* (straight-line)
approximation that increasingly *underestimates* the true price for big swings. The bigger the
move, the more you must add the convexity term to stay accurate. A perpetual bond paying \$50 a
year at a 6.25% yield is worth \$50 / 0.0625 = \$800 — and its price-yield relationship is a
*curve*, not the line duration alone would draw.
</Callout>

### When it matters

It matters most in *volatile* rate environments and for *long-duration* bonds, where yield swings are large and the straight line drifts furthest from the truth. Two bonds with identical duration but different convexity aren't equal: the more convex one gains more when rates fall and loses less when they rise — a free asymmetry any holder should want, and one that real bond prices reflect.

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="What to carry out of this topic">
- **A bond is a loan, not a share.** You're a lender owed fixed coupons plus the face value back at maturity. The **coupon is a fixed percent of face value**, so its cash never changes when the price moves — and you have no ownership upside or vote.
- **Price and yield move opposite ways (the seesaw).** Current yield $= \text{annual coupon} / \text{price}$. When rates rise, existing bonds' prices fall. Coupon rate equals yield **only at par**.
- **The yield curve is a snapshot** of one issuer's bonds across maturities at a single moment — not one bond over time. Normal slopes up; **inverted** (short above long) is a leading indicator that has *preceded* recessions but **guarantees** none.
- **Duration is rate sensitivity:** $\%\,\Delta\text{Price} \approx -D \times \Delta y$, a percent move per 1% yield change (not dollars). **Lower coupons and longer maturities mean longer duration** — more interest-rate risk.
- **Convexity is the curve duration's straight line misses:** $\%\,\Delta\text{Price} \approx -D \times \Delta y + \tfrac{1}{2} C (\Delta y)^2$. The square makes it **always positive — gains bigger, losses smaller** — and it matters most for **large** yield moves. Duration alone is *not* exact for big swings.
- **The traps, retired for good:** the coupon is on *face*, not price; *yields up means prices down*; the curve is a snapshot, not one bond, and inversion is a warning, not a verdict; duration is a *percent* multiplier and *lower* coupons lengthen it; convexity *helps* the holder.
